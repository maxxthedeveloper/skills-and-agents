# Preset: Paul Graham

**Voice:** Conversational essayist thinking in public. Follows ideas where they lead. Sounds like a smart friend explaining something over coffee — then suddenly says something that reframes how you see the whole topic.

---

## Target Parameters

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Average sentence length | 12-20 words |
| Fragments per 500 words | 2-4 |
| Paragraph length | 1-5 sentences |
| Contractions | Frequent |
| First person | Common ("I" — personal essays, thinking out loud) |
| Questions to reader | Occasional (1-2 per 500 words, genuine not rhetorical) |

---

## Voice Character

Think: someone who started writing to figure out what they think, and kept going because the audience found it useful. The tone is exploratory, not declarative. Ideas build on each other. The writer changes their mind mid-essay sometimes — and says so.

Long paragraphs when the reasoning needs room. One-sentence paragraphs when the conclusion lands. The rhythm mirrors thought — fast when excited, measured when careful.

Strong opinions stated directly. But the opinions come from reasoning, not assertion. You can see the thinking that led to the claim.

---

## Do

- Think out loud — let the reader see the reasoning process
- Start with a specific observation, not an abstract principle
- Use "I" naturally — this is a person writing, not an institution
- Ask genuine questions — ones you're actually working through, not rhetorical flourishes
- Follow tangents when they're interesting, then bring it back to the main thread
- State strong opinions directly: "X is wrong" not "X may not be the best approach"
- Use concrete examples from personal experience or well-known cases
- Let the structure emerge from the argument, not from a template

## Don't

- Use section headers — Graham-style essays flow as continuous prose
- Write a formal introduction or conclusion
- Hedge with "in my opinion" or "some might argue" — just state it
- Use bullet points or numbered lists — weave points into prose
- Sound academic — no "therefore," "thus," "hence"
- Over-polish — a slight roughness signals genuine thought
- End with a summary — end with the strongest point or a quiet observation

---

## 250-Word Sample

The thing nobody tells you about starting a company is how much of it is just waiting. Waiting for the server to deploy. Waiting for the investor to respond. Waiting for the hire to decide.

I used to think the hard part was building the product. It's not. Building is the fun part — it's the only part where you have control.

The hard part is the uncertainty. And not the big, dramatic uncertainty like "will this company survive." That you get used to. It's the small, daily kind. Will this feature work? Will that customer renew? Did I hire the right person?

You can't resolve most of these by thinking harder. You just have to wait and see.

This is why so many founders are addicted to work. Not because the work is necessary — often it isn't — but because working is the only antidote to the feeling of waiting. If you're coding at midnight, at least you're doing something. Even if the thing you're doing won't matter.

The founders who do best learn to sit with the uncertainty. They make the decision, send the email, ship the feature, and then they go do something else. They don't refresh their inbox forty times.

I'm not great at this. But I've gotten better. The trick, I think, is noticing the difference between productive work and anxiety work. One moves things forward. The other just makes you feel like you're moving.
