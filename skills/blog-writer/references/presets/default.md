# Preset: Default

**Voice:** A balanced blend that borrows each writer's voice at its strongest moment. Graham's exploratory thinking for ideas. Naval's compression for principles. Zinsser's warmth for personal moments. The result is a flexible, natural voice that adapts its register to what the paragraph needs.

---

## Target Parameters

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Average sentence length | 10-18 words |
| Fragments per 500 words | 2-3 |
| Paragraph length | 1-5 sentences |
| Contractions | Frequent |
| First person | Moderate (use "I" when sharing experience, drop it for universal claims) |
| Questions to reader | Occasional (1 per 500 words, genuine) |

---

## Voice Character

Think: a writer who has absorbed all three influences so thoroughly that they don't sound like any one of them. They sound like themselves. The voice shifts subtly depending on what the paragraph is doing:

- **Exploring an idea** → Graham mode: longer sentences, following the reasoning, thinking out loud
- **Stating a principle** → Naval mode: compressed, declarative, tweetable
- **Sharing experience** → Zinsser mode: warm, specific, personal details that ground the abstract

The shifts are invisible. The reader doesn't notice the gear changes because the voice stays consistent in tone — direct, honest, unshowy.

This is the default because it's the most versatile. It works for technical topics and personal ones, for 400-word posts and 1,200-word essays.

---

## Do

- Shift register based on what the paragraph needs — compression for principles, expansion for exploration
- Use "I" when you have a genuine personal stake; drop it when stating something universal
- Mix paragraph lengths freely — a one-sentence paragraph after a five-sentence one creates emphasis
- Include concrete specifics — names, numbers, compressed anecdotes
- State opinions directly but show the reasoning that got you there
- Let the strongest sentence in each section stand alone if possible
- Write a closer that earns its finality — the last line should feel like a resting place, not a summary

## Don't

- Lock into one register for the entire post — the default voice is defined by its flexibility
- Over-compress at the expense of warmth — Naval's staccato doesn't suit every paragraph
- Over-explore at the expense of pace — Graham's long reasoning chains need punctuation with short hits
- Write a "balanced take" that hedges everything — having a default voice doesn't mean having no opinion
- Use section headers unless the post genuinely needs them (most don't)
- Let the voice shift become noticeable — if the reader can spot where you switched modes, it's too abrupt

## 250-Word Sample

The best engineers I know share one trait that has nothing to do with code. They're comfortable saying "I don't know."

Not as a hedge. Not as false modesty. They genuinely don't know, and they say so, and then they go figure it out. The whole process takes less time than the alternative — which is pretending to know, building on a shaky foundation, and debugging the confusion later.

I watched this happen on a project last year. Two senior engineers disagreed about whether to use a message queue or direct API calls. One had strong opinions. The other said, "I'm not sure. Let me build both as prototypes this week and measure."

By Thursday, the answer was obvious. The message queue added 40ms of latency and three new failure modes. The direct API call was simpler, faster, and easier to debug. But the measurement revealed something neither of them expected — the actual bottleneck was neither the queue nor the API. It was the serialization layer. They'd been debating the wrong problem.

That's what "I don't know" buys you. Not ignorance. Clarity. When you admit what you don't know, you create space to find out what you actually need to know.

This is hard in practice. Teams reward confidence. Promotions go to people with answers, not questions. But the answers that come from genuine uncertainty are better than the ones that come from performing certainty.

Say "I don't know" more often. You'll be wrong less.
