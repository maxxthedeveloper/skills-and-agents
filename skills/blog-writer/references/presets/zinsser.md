# Preset: Zinsser

**Voice:** Warm clarity. Personal craft. A writer who cares about the reader's experience and treats writing as a deliberate act of communication. Clean but not cold. Precise but not clinical.

---

## Target Parameters

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Average sentence length | 12-18 words |
| Fragments per 500 words | 1-2 |
| Paragraph length | 2-5 sentences |
| Contractions | Moderate (natural, not forced) |
| First person | Frequent — "I" is how the writer stays honest |
| Questions to reader | Rare (max 1 per 500 words, never rhetorical filler) |

---

## Voice Character

Think: a veteran writer sharing something they learned through practice. Not lecturing — just telling you what they noticed. The warmth comes from specificity, not from enthusiasm. When the writer cares, you can tell — not because they say "this is exciting," but because the details get sharper.

Sentences are clean. Every word earns its place. But unlike Naval's compression, there's room to breathe. A metaphor is allowed if it illuminates. An aside is welcome if it's honest.

The "I" is present and unpretentious. This is someone who has done the work and is sharing what they found. No authority claims — just earned perspective.

---

## Do

- Use "I" naturally and frequently — it keeps the writing honest
- Include sensory details when they serve the point: sounds, textures, specific images
- Let sentences flow with natural rhythm — long ones for immersion, short ones for emphasis
- Show craft without showing off — clean transitions, precise verbs, no filler
- Include small personal details that ground the writing: "I was sitting in a coffee shop in Brooklyn when..."
- Be specific about the process — how you learned something, not just what you learned
- End with a quiet, earned observation — not a bang, not a whimper

## Don't

- Shout — no exclamation marks, no "incredible," no breathless enthusiasm
- Lecture — you're sharing, not teaching from a podium
- Use jargon without earning it — if a technical term is needed, explain it simply the first time
- Write generically — if you could swap in a different topic and the sentence still works, it's too abstract
- Apologize for writing about something personal — own it
- Over-compress — Zinsser values brevity but not at the cost of warmth
- End with a moral — trust the reader to draw their own conclusion

## 250-Word Sample

I learned to write by deleting. That sounds like a paradox, but it's not. Every writing teacher I had told me to add things. More detail. More examples. More transitions to "guide the reader." The readers didn't need guiding. They needed fewer words in their way.

The first piece I published was 3,000 words. The editor cut it to 1,200. I remember reading the shorter version on the train home, expecting to feel gutted. Instead I felt relief. Every sentence that survived was one I was proud of. The ones she cut were the ones I'd added to fill space — to sound like a writer instead of being one.

That distinction matters more than any writing rule. Sounding like a writer is performance. Being one is communication. The first asks: does this impress? The second asks: does this connect?

I still over-write in first drafts. Everyone does. The difference is I no longer believe the first draft is writing. It's thinking. The writing happens in the second pass, when you read each sentence and ask: is this earning its place?

Most sentences aren't. They're scaffolding — necessary for construction, useless in the finished building. The skill is learning to recognize them.

My editor taught me a test I still use. Cover a sentence with your hand. Read what comes before and after. If nothing is lost, delete the sentence.

I delete about a third of what I write. On good days, half.

The writing gets better every time something goes.
