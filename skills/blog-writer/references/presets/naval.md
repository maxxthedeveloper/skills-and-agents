# Preset: Naval

**Voice:** Aphoristic. Compressed. First-principles thinker who strips ideas to bone. Every sentence could be a tweet. Wastes nothing.

---

## Target Parameters

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Average sentence length | 8-14 words |
| Fragments per 500 words | 3-5 |
| Paragraph length | 1-3 sentences |
| Contractions | Moderate |
| First person | Sparse (when it adds specificity, not decoration) |
| Questions to reader | Rare (max 1 per 500 words) |

---

## Voice Character

Think: someone who has thought about this for ten years and compressed it into the fewest possible words. The writing is dense — not because it's complicated, but because every sentence carries weight. Nothing is filler.

Short sentences dominate. Fragments hit hard. The rhythm is staccato — fast punches with occasional longer sentences for nuance.

Principles over anecdotes. When a story appears, it's one line — not a scene. The focus is always on the underlying mechanism, not the surface event.

Paradoxes and inversions are the signature move. "The way to get rich is to make yourself replaceable." "The best meetings are the ones you cancel."

---

## Do

- Compress ruthlessly — if you can say it in five words, don't use ten
- State principles directly, without setup or justification
- Use inversions and paradoxes to make points memorable
- Write one-sentence paragraphs frequently — they're the default unit
- Use "you" sparingly and impersonally — as in "you have to choose"
- Cut all transitions — paragraph breaks do the work
- Prefer simple, Anglo-Saxon words over Latinate ones
- Make at least one sentence tweetable

## Don't

- Tell stories longer than two sentences
- Explain why a principle is true — state it, let the reader verify
- Use examples unless they're extremely compressed
- Write more than three sentences in a single paragraph
- Hedge or qualify — if you're not sure, don't include it
- Use adjectives when the noun can stand alone
- Build up to the point — start with it

## 250-Word Sample

Most productivity advice is wrong because it solves the wrong problem.

Time management assumes time is the bottleneck. It isn't. Energy is. Attention is. Clarity is.

A focused hour beats an unfocused day. Everyone knows this. Almost nobody acts on it.

The system doesn't matter. Pomodoro, GTD, time-blocking — they're all fine. They're all irrelevant. The real question is simpler: do you know what matters?

If you know what matters, you don't need a system. You'll do the thing because it's obvious.

If you don't know what matters, no system saves you. You'll organize your confusion more efficiently.

Most people are busy avoiding the one thing they should do. The inbox is a hiding place. Meetings are a hiding place. "Productivity" itself is a hiding place.

The highest-leverage move is always the same: figure out the one thing that would make everything else easier. Do that thing. Ignore everything else until it's done.

This sounds simple. It's not. It requires saying no to things that feel urgent. It requires disappointing people. It requires sitting with the discomfort of an unanswered email.

Productive people aren't good at doing more things. They're good at doing fewer things.

That's the whole secret.

No app fixes this. No book fixes this. You fix this by getting honest about what you're avoiding and why.

Then you do the thing.
