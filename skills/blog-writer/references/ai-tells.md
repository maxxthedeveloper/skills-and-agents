# AI Tells -- Zero Tolerance Reference

This file catalogs every known pattern, phrase, and habit that marks writing as AI-generated. Organized from most important (structural patterns) to most granular (individual words).

The goal is not just to avoid these tells. It's to understand WHY they're tells, so new violations that aren't on any list get caught too.

The master principle: **If it could have been generated by filling in a template, it's AI writing.** Real writing comes from a specific person thinking about a specific thing. Templates come from pattern-matching across millions of texts.

Why does AI default to generic? Because language models predict the most probable next token, which means every sentence gravitates toward the statistical average — regression to the mean. The output is a lossy compression of thought: structurally coherent but stripped of original density, like a JPEG that looks fine at thumbnail size but falls apart when you zoom in. Understanding this mechanism helps catch new tells that aren't on any list — if a sentence reads like high-probability tokens strung together, it probably is.

## Part 1: Structural Patterns

These matter more than any individual word. A post can avoid every banned phrase and still read as AI if these patterns are present.

### 1.1 The Negation-Pivot

**Pattern**: "It's not X. It's Y." / "What matters isn't X -- it's Y." / "The problem isn't X. The problem is Y."

**Why it's a tell**: It's a rhetorical template that produces the appearance of insight without actual thinking. You can apply it to literally any topic:
- "The problem isn't speed. It's direction."
- "What they need isn't more data. It's better questions."
- "It's not about working harder. It's about working on the right things."

The structure does the work, not the idea. Real insights don't need this scaffolding.

**Fix**: State the point directly. "Most people work hard enough. They just aim badly." No theatrical pivot needed.

Also watch for variants:
- "Not because X. Because Y."
- "You're not just X -- you're Y."
- "It's not just a product launch. It's a paradigm shift."
- "This isn't just X, it's Y."

### 1.2 The Rule of Three Compulsion

**Pattern**: Three adjectives, three examples, three bullet points, three sections. Everything grouped in threes with mechanical regularity.

**Why it's a tell**: The rhetorical Rule of Three is a real device, but AI uses it compulsively. When every enumeration has exactly three items, the writing is visibly templated.

**Fix**: Vary counts deliberately. Two items. Four items. Five. Sometimes one. Break the pattern.

### 1.3 Em Dash Addiction

**Pattern**: Em dashes in every other paragraph, always for parenthetical asides or emphasis.

**Why it's a tell**: Most humans learned to write through texting and email, where nobody types em dashes (there's no key for it). AI learned from professionally edited text where em dashes appear frequently. The gap between how often AI uses them and how often humans use them is enormous.

**Fix**: Max 2 per 500 words. Use commas, parentheses, or just restructure the sentence. If a parenthetical aside isn't important enough for its own sentence, it might not be important enough to include.

### 1.4 Uniform Rhythm

**Pattern**: Sentences of similar length throughout. Paragraphs of similar size. The same cadence repeated for pages.

**Why it's a tell**: Linguists call it "low burstiness." Human writing naturally mixes short punches with long flowing sentences. AI maintains a monotone rhythm because it optimizes for the statistically average sentence.

**Fix**: Deliberately vary sentence length. Short sentence. Then a longer one that builds and develops. Then a fragment. Then medium. The writing should sound like someone thinking, not like a metronome.

### 1.5 Structural Symmetry

**Pattern**: If section 1 has three paragraphs and a bullet list, section 2 also has three paragraphs and a bullet list. Every section mirrors the others.

**Why it's a tell**: Humans dwell on things that interest them and rush through the obvious. AI distributes information evenly because it has no genuine interest in any part of the topic.

**Fix**: Let sections vary in length and structure based on what each idea needs, not on visual symmetry.

### 1.6 Explanation Over Argument

**Pattern**: The post describes a topic from multiple angles without ever committing to a position. Covers "challenges" and "benefits" evenhandedly. Never says "this is wrong."

**Why it's a tell**: AI is trained to be balanced and avoid controversy. This produces writing that explains without arguing. But good blog posts argue. They have a take.

**Fix**: Commit to a position. Say what you actually think. If both sides have merit, pick the side you believe and argue for it honestly.

### 1.7 No Digressions

**Pattern**: The post stays perfectly on topic from start to finish. No asides, tangents, parenthetical thoughts, or personal references that break the structure.

**Why it's a tell**: Human thinking is messy. We make asides. We interrupt ourselves. We reference something tangentially related. AI follows a clean logical path because it never had a tangential thought.

**Fix**: Include a brief aside or digression if one naturally arises. Not every sentence needs to serve the thesis directly.

### 1.8 The "From X to Y" False Range

**Pattern**: "From intimate gatherings to global movements." "From seasoned professionals to curious beginners." "From bustling cities to serene countryside."

**Why it's a tell**: It implies a meaningful spectrum where none exists. It sounds analytical but says nothing.

**Fix**: Delete the construction entirely. Say the specific thing you mean.

### 1.9 Tailing "-ing" Clauses

**Pattern**: Sentences ending with empty participial phrases that claim significance without demonstrating it:
- "...emphasizing the significance of community engagement."
- "...reflecting the continued relevance of traditional values."
- "...highlighting the importance of sustainable practices."
- "...underscoring the need for further research."

**Why it's a tell**: These phrases editorialize without adding content. They're padding that inflates the importance of the preceding clause.

**Fix**: Delete the tailing clause. If the significance needs stating, give it its own sentence with concrete support.

### 1.10 Passive Voice Default

**Pattern**: AI defaults to passive constructions that hide who is doing what. "The decision was made to restructure the team" instead of "We cut the team in half." "It was determined that changes were needed" instead of "I saw the problem and fixed it."

**Why it's a tell**: Passive voice lets the writer avoid naming an actor. AI favors it because attributing actions to specific people requires knowledge it doesn't have. The result reads like a corporate memo where nobody is responsible for anything. Stephen King's zombie test: if you can add "by zombies" after the verb and the sentence still works grammatically ("The decision was made *by zombies*"), it's passive. Rewrite it.

**Fix**: Name the actor. Use active verbs. "We decided," "I built," "the team shipped." Active voice is the single most universally recommended rule across Strunk & White, Orwell, King, and Zinsser — and for good reason. It forces clarity about who did what.

### 1.11 Self-Answering Questions

**Pattern**: Posing a question and immediately answering it in the next sentence. "So what does this mean? It means..."

**Why it's a tell**: It's a presentation technique, not a writing technique. In writing, the question-then-answer format feels performative.

**Fix**: Just make the statement. Skip the rhetorical question.

### 1.12 Manufactured Epiphanies

**Pattern**: Sentences structured to feel like revelations but that are really just reframings of the previous paragraph. "And that's the real lesson." "That changes everything." "Think about what that means."

**Why it's a tell**: Real insights don't need to announce themselves. If the reader needs to be told something is profound, it probably isn't.

**Fix**: Delete the announcement. Let the idea land on its own.

### 1.13 Slop Formulas

These specific constructions are AI fingerprints:

- "Here's the thing" / "Here's the thing:"
- "Let that sink in"
- "And that's okay"
- "This matters because"
- "[Noun]. That's it. That's the [thing]."
- "The uncomfortable truth is"
- "Not because X. Because Y."
- Opening with "So" or "Look,"
- "All the X. None of the Y."
- "No X. No Y. No Z." (staccato triplet negation)

## Part 2: Voice and Tone Patterns

### 2.1 The Helpful Explainer

**Pattern**: The writing sounds like a knowledgeable but overenthusiastic teaching assistant. Thorough, balanced, polite, and completely without personality.

**Why it's a tell**: This is Claude's default voice. Detailed, explanatory, context-rich answers with a formal-yet-friendly tone. It's what you get when you average billions of web pages.

**Fix**: Write from a specific vantage point. Have opinions. Be willing to be wrong. A specific person with specific experience, not a generic knowledgeable narrator.

### 2.2 Both-Sides-ism

**Pattern**: Every position gets the "to be fair" treatment. "While X has its merits, Y also offers compelling advantages." Never picks a side.

**Why it's a tell**: Trained to avoid controversy. Real experts have stances.

**Fix**: Pick a side. Argue for it. Acknowledge counterarguments briefly and specifically, not with generic fairness padding.

### 2.3 Performative Enthusiasm

**Pattern**: "This is truly exciting!" "What a fascinating development!" Everything is "incredible," "remarkable," "fascinating."

**Why it's a tell**: Empty superlatives without earned specificity. The writer hasn't demonstrated why something is exciting -- they've just labeled it so.

**Fix**: Show, don't tell. Describe the thing. If it's genuinely interesting, the reader will feel it without being told.

### 2.4 Hedging Everything

**Pattern**: Multiple qualifiers stacked on every claim. "It could potentially be somewhat beneficial in certain circumstances." Where a human would just say "it helps."

**Why it's a tell**: AI hedges because its training penalizes confident claims. But good writing commits.

**Fix**: State the claim. Then qualify with specifics if needed. "This works for teams under 20. Above that, it breaks down" is better than "This might work for some teams depending on their specific circumstances."

### 2.5 Adjective Stacking

**Pattern**: Multiple adjectives piled in front of a noun, each meaning roughly the same thing. "A powerful, flexible, intuitive platform." "An innovative, cutting-edge, transformative solution." Three adjectives that all resolve to "good."

**Why it's a tell**: AI stacks adjectives because each one adds positive sentiment probability without requiring actual information. It's the cheapest way to make a sentence sound impressive. But stacking vague positives doesn't compound — it dilutes. The reader remembers none of them.

**Fix**: Delete all but one adjective, or replace the entire cluster with a concrete fact. "A powerful, flexible, intuitive platform" becomes "A platform that handles 10,000 concurrent users on a $50/month plan." One fact beats three adjectives every time.

### 2.6 Performative Emotion

**Pattern**: AI labels emotions instead of showing them. "The team was devastated by the outage." "She was incredibly passionate about the mission." "It was a deeply moving experience."

**Why it's a tell**: Naming an emotion is the opposite of evoking it. When you write "the team was devastated," the reader processes the word and moves on. When you write "three engineers sat in a conference room at 2 AM, laptops open, nobody talking," the reader *feels* it. AI labels because generating emotional labels is statistically easy. Showing requires specific, observed detail that AI doesn't have.

**Fix**: Replace emotion labels with observable details. Use sensory language — what you'd see, hear, or feel if you were in the room. Let the reader supply the emotion.

| Before (labels) | After (shows) |
|-----------------|---------------|
| The team was devastated by the outage. | Three engineers stared at the dashboard at 2 AM. Nobody spoke. The Slack channel had 200 unread messages. |
| She was incredibly passionate about the mission. | She quit a six-figure job, maxed out two credit cards, and slept in the office for a month. |
| It was a deeply moving experience. | My hands were shaking when I closed the laptop. I sat there for ten minutes before I could stand up. |

## Part 3: Banned Phrases

Scan is case-insensitive. Partial matches count. Zero tolerance -- every instance must be eliminated.

### 3.1 Throat-Clearing Openers

These waste the reader's time before the real point arrives.

- In today's world / In today's rapidly evolving / In today's fast-paced / In today's digital age
- In an era of
- In the ever-changing landscape / In the ever-evolving landscape
- Have you ever wondered
- Are you tired of
- Imagine a world where / Imagine this / Picture this
- Let me tell you something
- Here's the thing:
- Let's be honest
- It's no secret that
- We've all been there
- When it comes to
- In the realm of / In the world of

**What to do instead**: Start with a fact, a number, a name, or an action. The first sentence should earn its place.

### 3.2 Filler Transitions

These exist only to bridge paragraphs. If the next paragraph doesn't flow without them, the structure is wrong.

- Let's dive in / Let's dive deeper / Let's unpack this
- Let's explore / Let's break this down / Let's take a closer look
- With that in mind / With that being said / That being said / Having said that
- So, what does this mean? / But here's the kicker / But wait, there's more
- Now, here's where it gets interesting
- Here's the bottom line / So, what's the takeaway?
- Building on this idea / Another important consideration is
- And honestly? / And truthfully?

**What to do instead**: Delete them. The paragraph break is the transition. If it doesn't flow, reorder the paragraphs.

### 3.3 Summary Closers

End with the strongest point, not a recap.

- In conclusion / To sum up / To summarize / In summary / In essence
- All in all / At the end of the day / The bottom line is
- So there you have it / And that's the beauty of
- The takeaway here is / What's clear is that / The lesson is clear
- If there's one thing to remember / Final thoughts
- Overall / To put it simply

**What to do instead**: End with the last idea that earns its place. The reader doesn't need a recap of a 600-word post.

### 3.4 Empty Emphasis

If the sentence doesn't work without the intensifier, the sentence doesn't work.

- incredibly / extremely / absolutely / truly / really / simply / very
- quite frankly / needless to say / it goes without saying
- make no mistake / the reality is / the truth is / the fact of the matter is
- undeniably / unquestionably / remarkably

### 3.5 Corporate and Buzzword Vocabulary

These words signal marketing copy, not human writing.

- leverage / robust / seamless / seamlessly / empower
- scalable / cutting-edge / game-changer / game-changing
- best-in-class / world-class / synergy / paradigm shift
- holistic / ecosystem / innovative / revolutionize
- streamline / optimize / elevate / transformative
- actionable insights / thought leadership
- at the forefront / groundbreaking / pioneering
- unparalleled / breathtaking

**What to do instead**: Use plain verbs. Built. Cut. Shipped. Tested. Broke. Fixed. Grew.

### 3.6 Hedging Phrases

State the claim, then qualify with specifics.

- It's important to note that / It's worth noting that / It should be noted that
- It's crucial to understand that / It's essential to recognize that
- One could argue that / One might argue that
- There are several factors to consider
- This is particularly important because
- While there's no one-size-fits-all
- The answer isn't straightforward
- Results may vary depending on / It depends on your specific needs

### 3.7 AI Sentence Starters

Start with the subject, not a transition adverb.

- Moreover / Furthermore / Additionally / Indeed
- Essentially, / Interestingly, / Importantly, / Notably,
- Significantly, / Fundamentally, / Ultimately, / Crucially,
- Subsequently / Accordingly / Consequently

### 3.8 The "Delve" Family and AI-isms

- delve / delving / deep dive / dive deep
- navigate challenges / navigate the complexities
- landscape (when describing a field or industry)
- tapestry / rich tapestry
- at the forefront / realm
- the intricacies of / foster / harness the power
- nuanced / multifaceted / intricate
- pivotal / compelling / comprehensive
- captivate / captivating / noteworthy
- testament / testament to / stands as a testament
- underscore / underscores / showcasing
- embark / embarking / unveil / unveiling
- beacon / symphony (metaphorical) / enigma
- vibrant / bustling / metropolis / nestled
- watershed / indelible / steadfast / unwavering
- ever-evolving / rich cultural heritage

### 3.9 Copula-Avoidance Substitutes

AI avoids simple "is/has" by reaching for fancier alternatives.

- serves as / features / boasts / showcases
- highlights / underscores / speaks volumes

**What to do instead**: Just say "is" or "has." "The API is fast" beats "The API boasts impressive speed."

### 3.10 Hollow Significance Phrases

These inflate importance without demonstrating it.

- plays a vital/crucial/pivotal/significant role
- stands as a reminder / leaves a lasting impact
- continues to captivate / enduring legacy / lasting legacy
- rich tapestry of / deeply rooted / profound heritage
- complex and multifaceted (700x more common in AI text than human text)
- intricate interplay (100x more common)
- played a crucial role (70x more common)

## Part 4: Vocabulary

### The Plain Words Principle

If a simpler word means the same thing, use the simpler word. Every time.

| Fancy (don't use) | Plain (use this) |
|---|---|
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help |
| demonstrate | show |
| implement | build, do, set up |
| commence | start, begin |
| endeavor | try |
| necessitate | need |
| elucidate | explain |
| articulate | say, describe |
| cognizant | aware |
| ascertain | find out |
| subsequent | next, later |
| paramount | important, key |
| myriad | many |
| plethora | a lot, many |
| proliferation | spread, growth |
| dichotomy | split, difference |
| juxtaposition | contrast |
| paradigm | model, way, approach |
| synthesize | combine, bring together |
| methodology | method, approach |
| infrastructure | setup, system |
| trajectory | path, direction |
| encompasses | includes, covers |
| aforementioned | (just name the thing again) |
| wherein | where |
| thereby | so, which |
| henceforth | from now on |
| notwithstanding | despite |
| pertaining to | about |

The test: would you say this word out loud to a friend explaining the same idea? If not, use the word you'd actually say.

## Part 5: Formatting Tells

### Bold Abuse
AI bolds key terms with no clear logic. The **Term:** Definition bullet structure is a known AI signature. Avoid bold-colon-definition formatting in running prose.

### Header Overuse
Short posts don't need H2 sections. Many blog posts work better as continuous prose. Only use headers when a genuine topic shift occurs and the reader needs a visual break.

### Numbered Lists for Everything
Not everything is a list. If the items relate to each other and build an argument, weave them into prose. Lists are for genuinely independent items.

### Perfect Consistency
AI always uses the Oxford comma, always American English, never sentence fragments, never starts with "And" or "But." This perfection is itself a tell. Real writing has inconsistencies.

### Title Conventions
- No colons in titles ("Why X: A Comprehensive Guide" is AI-coded)
- No "Comprehensive Guide" or "Deep Dive" or "Everything You Need to Know"
- Short, direct titles. "Simplicity Wins" not "Why Simplicity is the Key to Winning in Modern Software Development"

## Scan Rules

1. Case-insensitive matching
2. Partial match -- "delving into the topic" triggers "delve"
3. Word-boundary aware -- "moreover" in a compound word doesn't trigger, but "Moreover," at sentence start does
4. Context-aware for "landscape" -- only flag when used metaphorically for a field/industry, not literal landscapes
5. Zero tolerance -- a single remaining banned phrase fails the scan
6. No circular substitution -- replacing "leverage" with "harness" is not a fix; both are banned

---

## The Meta-Principle

After scanning for all of the above, ask one final question:

**Does this read like it was written by a specific person with specific experience and specific opinions? Or does it read like it was generated by averaging a million blog posts?**

Orwell's test: "Good prose should be transparent, like a window pane." If the reader notices the writing instead of the ideas, the writing failed.

If the second, the problem isn't any individual phrase. It's that the writing lacks a genuine point of view. Go back to Phase 1 and find the real idea.
