# Type B: Scattered / Too Many Things

> "I have so much to do."

Everything feels urgent. They are bouncing between tasks, tabs, and ideas. They may look busy but nothing is getting finished. The problem is not productivity. The problem is that no single target is vivid enough to hold the ADHD brain's attention against the pull of everything else.

---

## 1. Why This Happens: The Neuroscience

### The Interest-Based Nervous System

Dr. William Dodson coined this term to describe a fundamental difference in ADHD motivation. Neurotypical brains run on an *importance-based* nervous system — they can force themselves to do things because "it matters." ADHD brains run on an *interest-based* nervous system, driven by four activation triggers:

1. **Novelty** — it's new
2. **Urgency** — the deadline is *now*
3. **Challenge** — it's a puzzle or a game
4. **Personal interest** — it connects to something they genuinely care about

If a task hits none of these triggers, the ADHD brain *cannot* prioritize it, no matter how important it is. When facing a list of 8 tasks, the brain rapidly scans all 8 for these triggers, and if multiple tasks ping different triggers (this one is novel, that one is urgent, that one is interesting), the attentional system oscillates between them. It cannot choose because multiple targets are equally "bright."

### Dopamine and Novelty-Seeking

Stimulus novelty is a potent trigger for dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra. Novel stimuli produce brief dopamine spikes that bias the brain toward exploration over exploitation. This is adaptive for learning but catastrophic for task completion.

In ADHD, this novelty-seeking is amplified by genetic factors — particularly DRD4 receptor polymorphisms. The result:

- **Switching tasks *feels* productive** because each new task produces a small dopamine hit from novelty.
- **Staying on one task produces diminishing dopamine** as novelty fades.
- **The brain actively resists commitment** to a single task because commitment means accepting that all other tasks (and their dopamine potential) are temporarily off-limits.

### The Attentional Spotlight Problem

Huberman describes dopamine as creating a "cone" of attention — narrowing visual and auditory focus onto a single target. In the scattered state, this cone is too wide. The brain is trying to attend to everything, which means it is attending to nothing effectively.

The prefrontal cortex in ADHD shows reduced capacity to coordinate with the default-mode network (the "mind-wandering" system). Under task load, background noise increases instead of decreasing. The person experiences this as *mental chatter* — a dozen half-formed plans competing for attention simultaneously.

### Decision Paralysis Is Real

Research shows that 82% of ADHD participants report frequent difficulties with decision-making, and 68% say decision paralysis significantly affects work performance. The mechanism:

- **Every task on the list requires a "pick me" decision.**
- **Each decision draws on the same depleted executive function pool.**
- **Fear of choosing wrong amplifies emotional dysregulation.**
- **Result: zero tasks started instead of one imperfect choice.**

This is why "just pick one" works better than "prioritize your list." Prioritizing requires comparing all options simultaneously — exactly the cognitive operation that is broken.

## 2. Steve Jobs: Focus Means Saying No

> "People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas that there are. You have to pick carefully. I'm actually as proud of the things we haven't done as the things I have done. Innovation is saying no to 1,000 things."

### The Apple Product Matrix

When Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, the company had over 30 products — printers, PDAs, dozens of Mac variants. Revenue was dropping. Engineers were spread across too many projects.

Jobs did not reorganize priorities. He *eliminated 70% of the product line*. He drew a 2x2 grid: Consumer/Pro x Desktop/Portable. Four products. That's it. He killed the Newton PDA. He killed printers. He killed products that were selling.

The lesson is not about product management. It is about the *psychological relief of elimination*. Apple's team could finally focus because there was only one thing to focus on in each quadrant. The cognitive load of "which project matters?" was zeroed out.

### Application to the Scattered Person

The person with 8 tasks is Apple in 1997 — spread too thin, declining output, no single thing getting real attention.

The intervention is not to help them prioritize all 8. It is to help them **eliminate or defer 5-7 of them**, so the remaining 1-2 can receive full attention.

**Coach line:** "You've got 8 tasks. Steve Jobs would kill 6 of them. Which 2 actually matter today? Everything else moves to tomorrow."

## 3. Paul Graham: Type-B Procrastination

> "Unless you're working on the biggest thing you could be working on, you're type-B procrastinating."

### The Most Dangerous Kind

Graham identifies three types of procrastination:
- **Type A:** You do nothing.
- **Type B:** You do less important things instead of the most important thing.
- **Type C:** You work on something bigger and bolder than what's "supposed" to be done.

**Type B is the most dangerous because it feels productive.** The person is checking off tasks, responding to messages, organizing files, clearing small items. Their day *looks* busy. They may even *feel* productive. But the one thing that would actually move the needle — the big task, the scary task, the important task — sits untouched.

Graham calls this "unconscious type-B procrastination" because the person often does not realize they are doing it. The busyness acts as a psychological shield against the discomfort of the real work.

### Application

For the scattered person, the question is not "what should I do first?" but:

**"What's the biggest thing you could be working on right now?"**

If the answer is not what they've been doing, they are type-B procrastinating. Name it directly.

**Coach line:** "You've been productive today — on the wrong things. The biggest thing on your list is [X]. Everything else is a shield. What would it take to spend 30 minutes on [X] right now?"

## 4. Peter Thiel: The Power Law of Tasks

### Not All Tasks Are Equal

> "We don't live in a normal world; we live under a power law."

Thiel's power law observation about venture capital applies perfectly to task lists: one task will matter more than all the others combined. The scattered person treats their list as if all items have equal weight. They don't.

In any given day:
- **1 task will create 80% of the meaningful progress.** This is the power-law task.
- **2-3 tasks are supporting work** — necessary but not transformative.
- **The remaining 4-5 tasks are noise** — admin, maintenance, things that feel urgent but aren't important.

### "Competition Is For Losers"

Thiel's most provocative idea: competing in crowded markets destroys value. Applied to task management: **competing tasks destroy focus.** When 8 tasks compete for your attention, all of them lose. The solution is not better competition (prioritization) — it is to create a monopoly of attention on one task.

### The Definite Optimist

> "A definite person determines the one best thing to do and then does it."

The scattered person is operating in *indefinite* mode — "I'll work on whatever feels right." The intervention is to shift them to *definite* mode — "I have decided that this one thing is what matters, and I am doing it now."

**Coach line:** "One of these tasks matters 10x more than the rest. Which one? That's your only job for the next hour. Let the rest be noise."

### The One-Task Question

Adapted from Gary Keller's The ONE Thing but with Thiel's power-law framing:

**"What's the one thing that, if you did it, would make everything else either easier or unnecessary?"**

This question works because it reframes the scattered feeling from "I have to do everything" to "most of this might not even matter if I do the right thing first."

## 5. Tony Robbins: The Focus Triad

> "Where focus goes, energy flows."

### Why the Scattered Person Is Stuck

The scattered person's focus is on **the pile** — the mass of undone things. This produces:
- Cortisol (stress from perceived overwhelm)
- Decision fatigue (which one? which one?)
- Paralysis (frozen by the magnitude of "everything")

Energy is flowing toward *anxiety about tasks* rather than toward *executing a task*. The intervention is a deliberate focus shift.

### The Triad Applied

**1. Physiology (do this first):**
Stand up. Change position. Move to a different room or spot. The physical break disrupts the overwhelm-paralysis loop. You cannot feel scattered and physically grounded at the same time.

**2. Focus (the pivot):**
Shift attention from the pile to ONE outcome. Not one task on the list — one *outcome* you want to produce in the next 30 minutes.
- "In 30 minutes, I will have [specific deliverable]."
- Everything else ceases to exist for those 30 minutes.
- This is not prioritization. This is *attentional triage*. One patient. Everyone else waits.

**3. Language (the lock):**
Change internal language from overwhelm-mode to action-mode:
- Not "I have so much to do" but "I'm doing ONE thing right now."
- Not "I'll never get through all this" but "I don't need to. I need to finish THIS."
- Not "everything is urgent" but "this is the only thing that exists for 30 minutes."

### RPM for the Scattered State

When someone has 8 tasks and can't pick, RPM cuts through the noise:

**Result:** "What's the ONE outcome that would make you feel like today was worthwhile — even if nothing else gets done?"

**Purpose:** "Why does that one matter most? What would completing it mean for you?"

**Massive Action Plan:** "What are the 3 moves to get it done? Just that one thing."

The other 7 tasks are not eliminated forever. They are *parked*. The brain needs permission to let them go temporarily.

## 6. The Eisenhower Insight for ADHD Brains

### Urgency Bias

The classic Eisenhower Matrix separates tasks by Urgent/Not Urgent and Important/Not Important. For neurotypical brains, this is a useful sorting tool. For ADHD brains, it has a critical flaw:

**Everything feels equally urgent to an ADHD brain.**

This is because:
- **Time blindness collapses future deadlines into "now."** A task due in 3 days and a task due in 3 hours feel the same.
- **Emotional urgency overrides logical urgency.** A new email feels urgent because it is novel. The quarterly report does not feel urgent because it is familiar and boring.
- **The ADHD brain does not naturally distinguish importance from urgency.** The signal for "this matters" and the signal for "this is loud" use the same neural pathway.

### The ADHD-Adapted Intervention

Don't ask "what's urgent?" (everything is). Don't ask "what's important?" (they can't feel importance without interest).

Instead, ask:

**"If you could only finish ONE thing today, and you'd feel genuinely proud of yourself tonight — which one?"**

This bypasses the urgency/importance matrix entirely and activates the one ADHD motivator that can override everything else: *personal meaning*. Pride, identity, emotional satisfaction — these produce dopamine. "Important" does not.

### Alternative: The Forcing Function

If they truly cannot pick, remove the decision from them:

**"I'm going to pick for you. Based on what I can see, [task X] is the one that matters. You have 30 minutes. Go."**

This works because it eliminates the decision-making step entirely, which is the actual bottleneck. The ADHD brain is not bad at executing — it is bad at choosing under conditions of overwhelm.

## 7. Coaching Scripts

### Light Touch (first-time scatter, mild overwhelm)

> "Pick one. Any one. Wrong is better than none."

*If they hesitate:*

> "You're trying to pick the *right* one. There is no right one. There's only the one you start. Which one would feel good to finish?"

### Medium (moderate scatter, they know they're bouncing)

> "Your brain is bouncing because nothing on the list is compelling enough to hold it. That's not a discipline problem — that's a targeting problem."

> "Which of these would you be *proud* to finish today? Not 'should finish.' Would feel genuinely proud. That's your one."

*After they pick:*

> "Good. Everything else moves to tomorrow. Not 'later today.' Tomorrow. For the next [30/45/60] minutes, this is the only task that exists."

### Heavy (chronic scatter, productivity theater, recurring pattern)

> "You've got [X] tasks and you're working on zero of them. I can see the pattern — you bounce between things all day and nothing actually gets done. That's not a planning problem. That's your brain refusing to commit because committing means accepting you can't do everything today."

> "Here's the truth: you were never going to do all [X] of these. That was a fantasy. The question is whether you'll finish ONE thing today or ZERO things today."

> "Pick the one that matters. The one that moves your life forward, not just your inbox. Everything else waits. Not 'maybe later' — OFF THE TABLE for today."

*After they pick:*

> "[Task name]. [X] minutes. Starting now. The other [X-1] tasks don't exist until this one is done."

### Busyness-Calling Script (Type-B procrastination detected)

> "I can see you've been busy today — [list 2-3 small completed tasks]. That's real work. But [big task] hasn't been touched. Everything you did today was easier than [big task]. That's not a coincidence."

> "You're type-B procrastinating. You're staying busy to avoid the one thing that actually matters. The busyness feels productive, but it's a shield."

> "30 minutes on [big task]. Not to finish it. Just to break the seal. What's the first real action?"

### The Direct-Pick Script (when they genuinely cannot choose)

> "You can't pick, and that's okay. I'll pick for you."

> "Based on what I can see, [task X] is the highest-leverage thing on your list. It's the one that makes everything else easier if you do it first."

> "You have 30 minutes. Start now. If I'm wrong about the pick, you'll know within 5 minutes and you can switch. But you have to start SOMETHING in the next 60 seconds."

## 8. The Clearing Ritual

This is a tactical intervention for the moment of overwhelm. It takes 3-5 minutes and creates immediate psychological relief.

### Step 1: Brain Dump (2 minutes)

Write everything down. Every task, worry, idea, obligation. Not organized. Not prioritized. Just out of the head and onto paper or screen. The act of externalizing reduces working memory load instantly.

**Why this works neurologically:** Working memory is trying to hold all these items simultaneously, which is why the person feels "full" and paralyzed. Externalizing them frees the buffer.

### Step 2: The Cross-Out (1 minute)

Look at the list. Cross out everything that:
- Does not *need* to happen today
- Would not matter if it slipped to tomorrow
- Is someone else's problem
- Is maintenance/admin that can wait

**Be aggressive.** Most people cross out 1-2 items. Push for crossing out 60-70% of the list. The visual act of eliminating — the physical line through items — creates a neurological relief response. The pile shrinks. The overwhelm lifts.

### Step 3: Circle the One (30 seconds)

Of what remains, circle ONE item. The one that matters most. The one they would be proud to finish.

**Do not circle three. Circle one.** Multiple circles recreate the scatter. One circle creates a target.

### Step 4: Schedule It Now

Open Focuh (or calendar, or timer). Block the next 30-60 minutes for ONLY that circled task. Everything else is invisible.

### Why the Ritual Works

- **Externalization** reduces cognitive load
- **Elimination** reduces emotional overwhelm
- **Selection** creates a single target for the servo-mechanism
- **Scheduling** eliminates the "when do I start?" decision
- **The physical act** (writing, crossing, circling) engages the body, not just the mind — which is why it works better than "thinking about what to do"

**Coach line:** "Grab a piece of paper. Write everything that's in your head. All of it. Then cross out everything that doesn't absolutely need to happen today. What's left? Circle one. That's your next hour."

## 9. ADHD-Specific Tactical Angles

### The Commitment Problem

ADHD brains resist committing to one task because:
- **Commitment feels like loss.** Choosing task A means "losing" tasks B-H. Loss aversion is amplified by emotional dysregulation.
- **Commitment kills novelty.** Once you commit, the chosen task is no longer new. The dopamine drops. The un-chosen tasks, still untouched, retain their novelty glow.
- **Commitment requires sustained attention.** The ADHD brain knows (from painful experience) that it will struggle to stay on one thing. So it avoids committing as a protection against the shame of drifting.

**The reframe:** Commitment is not permanent. It is a 30-minute experiment. "Try this one for 30 minutes. If your brain rebels, we'll reassess." This lowers the stakes of choosing and gives the brain a known exit point.

### The Body Timer

ADHD time blindness means "work on this for 30 minutes" is not experienced as a bounded period. 30 minutes can feel like either 5 minutes or 3 hours.

**Always use a physical timer.** Phone timer, kitchen timer, visible countdown. The external timer replaces the broken internal clock. When time is *visible*, the brain can orient to it.

### Task Switching Is Not Progress

The scattered person may argue: "But I AM working. I'm just doing different things." Name the pattern directly:

**"Switching between tasks is not the same as making progress on one. Every switch costs you 10-25 minutes of refocus time. You've switched 6 times today. That's potentially hours of lost depth. Staying on one thing for 30 minutes will produce more than 3 hours of bouncing."**

This is backed by research on "attention residue" — when you switch tasks, part of your attention remains on the previous task, degrading performance on the current one. For ADHD brains with already-limited attentional bandwidth, this cost is even higher.

### The "Good Enough" Permission

Scattered people are often perfectionists in disguise. They bounce between tasks because none of them feel "ready" to commit to. They are waiting for the *right* moment, the *right* approach, the *right* amount of energy.

**"There is no right moment. There is only this moment. Start ugly. Start wrong. Start incomplete. The task does not need to be perfect — it needs to be *started*."**

## 10. Misdiagnosis Signals

### Actually Type A (Each Task Is Also Vague)

- They can't pick one because *none of them feel actionable*.
- When pressed to choose, they say "but I don't even know what to do on that one."
- The scatter is a symptom of having 8 vague projects instead of 8 clear tasks.
- **Test:** "Pick any task. What's the first 15-minute action on it?" If they can't answer for ANY task, the problem is not scatter — it's that every item needs the Type A breakdown first.

### Actually Type D (Low Energy Making Everything Heavy)

- The scatter is *listless*, not *frenetic*. They're not bouncing with energy — they're drifting with none.
- They say "too much to do" but the real signal is "I don't have the energy for any of it."
- Check WHOOP. If recovery is red/yellow and they seem flat, the intervention is rest and state change, not task selection.
- **Test:** "If you had full energy right now, would you know which task to start?" If yes, it's Type D.

### Actually Type E (Fear of Committing to the Real Work)

- One task on the list is significantly bigger/scarier than the others.
- The scatter serves as avoidance of *that specific task* — the others are shields.
- They keep "almost" getting to the important task but finding reasons to do other things first.
- **Test:** "Is there one task on this list that makes your stomach tighten when you think about it?" If yes, the scatter is a symptom of the fear. Switch to Type E.

### Actually Type C (Productive Procrastination)

- They are completing tasks — just the wrong ones.
- The scatter is not aimless; it is *strategically* aimed at avoiding one specific task.
- This looks like scatter but is actually avoidance with better camouflage.
- **Test:** "Which task have you touched the LEAST today?" If it's the most important one, it's Type C avoidance wearing a Type B mask.

## 11. The One-Liner Summary

**Eight tasks and zero progress means the problem isn't the tasks — it's the absence of a single, vivid, committed target. Pick one. Kill the rest for today. Thirty minutes of depth beats eight hours of breadth every time.**
