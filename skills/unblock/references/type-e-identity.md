# Type E: Fear / Identity Block

> "Something about this task specifically scares me or feels wrong."

This is the deepest block. The task is not too big, not too vague, not competing with other tasks, and the body has energy. The person knows what to do and has the capacity to do it. But they can't move. Something invisible is holding them back — and that something is their self-image.

The task represents a threshold. Doing it means becoming someone they haven't been yet. And part of them — usually the part installed by old experiences — doesn't believe they're allowed to cross.

---

## 1. Maxwell Maltz: The Self-Image Thermostat

### The Core Mechanism

Maltz's central discovery in Psycho-Cybernetics: the self-image is a thermostat. Just as a room thermostat keeps the temperature within a set range regardless of what you want, the self-image keeps behavior within a range that matches "who you believe you are."

- If you believe you're "someone who can't finish things," the thermostat will sabotage every project at the 80% mark.
- If you believe you're "not a real designer / writer / entrepreneur," the thermostat will create blocks on tasks that would prove you are.
- If you believe you "don't deserve success at this level," the thermostat will introduce avoidance, self-sabotage, or inexplicable paralysis exactly when the opportunity is closest.

**This is not willpower failure. It is the servo-mechanism faithfully pursuing its programmed target — which happens to be a limiting self-image.**

### The Servo-Mechanism

The mind is a goal-seeking system. When given a clear, vivid target that the self-image accepts, it self-corrects toward it automatically. Hyperfocus, flow, effortless productivity — these are the servo-mechanism locked on.

When the target conflicts with the self-image, the mechanism jams. This is what Maltz calls **purpose tremor**: the harder you try, the worse it gets. Like a hand trembling as it tries to thread a needle. Over-trying when the self-image is the blocker produces anxiety, paralysis, and avoidance — not progress.

**The paradox:** "trying harder" on a Type E block makes it worse. The intervention is not more effort. It is changing the target image the mechanism is pursuing.

### Dehypnotization

Maltz's term for the process of removing false beliefs about yourself. His argument: most self-image limitations are not based on reality. They are based on interpretations of past experiences — often childhood experiences — that were "installed" like hypnotic suggestions.

A teacher who said "you're not a math person." A parent who said "don't get too big for your britches." A first boss who said "you're not ready for this." These get internalized not as opinions but as facts about the self. They function like post-hypnotic suggestions — operating below conscious awareness, shaping behavior automatically.

**Dehypnotization questions:**
- "When did you first learn you couldn't do this?"
- "Whose voice is that? Is it yours, or someone who told you this about yourself?"
- "If that person was wrong — if that was their limitation projected onto you — what would be true instead?"

One question is usually enough in an unblock session. The full dehypnotization process belongs in `/reflect`. But naming the installed belief — even briefly — loosens its grip enough to start the task.

### Theatre of the Mind

Maltz's visualization technique: before attempting the task, spend 30 seconds vividly imagining yourself doing it successfully. Not finishing it. *Doing* it. See yourself sitting at the desk, writing the first paragraph, designing the first screen, making the first call. See it going well. See yourself calm, capable, in control.

This works because the servo-mechanism does not distinguish between a vividly imagined experience and a real one. A vivid mental rehearsal of success updates the self-image — even slightly — in the direction of "someone who does this." That slight update can be enough to release the purpose tremor.

**For ADHD brains:** Keep it short (30 seconds, not 5 minutes). Make it sensory (what do you see, hear, feel — not abstract). Do it immediately before starting, not hours earlier.

## 2. Tony Robbins: The Dickens Process (Adapted for 5 Minutes)

### The Full Concept

Robbins' Dickens Process is a 45-minute guided visualization that leverages pain and pleasure to break through limiting beliefs. Inspired by Dickens' *A Christmas Carol* — seeing the ghosts of past, present, and future to create emotional leverage for change.

His core principle: **"The strongest force in the human personality is the need to remain consistent with how we define ourselves."** People will sabotage success, relationships, and health to stay consistent with their identity. The Dickens Process creates enough emotional pain around the current identity that the person becomes willing to change it.

### The 5-Minute Adaptation

In an unblock session, you don't have 45 minutes. But you can create leverage in 60-90 seconds:

**Pain lever (30 seconds):**
> "If you keep avoiding this kind of work — tasks that require you to be visible, to put your real work out there — for the next 6 months, what does that cost you? Not abstractly. Specifically. What don't you have that you want? What do you feel about yourself?"

*Let them feel it for 10 seconds. Don't rush past the discomfort.*

> "Now stretch it. A year of this pattern. Two years. What does your life look like?"

**Pleasure lever (30 seconds):**
> "Now: what opens up if you break through it today? Not the task — the *pattern*. If you prove to yourself right now that you CAN do this kind of work, what does that make possible?"

**The pivot to action (immediately after):**
> "Both of those futures are real. The only thing that determines which one you get is what you do in the next 15 minutes. What's the smallest version of [TASK NAME] that proves you're the person who does this?"

### When to Use the Dickens Adaptation

- The block has appeared 3+ times on similar tasks.
- The person can articulate the fear ("what if it's not good enough," "what if they judge me").
- Standard interventions (pattern interrupt, state change, micro-task) have failed on this task.
- Recovery is green — this is not a body problem.

**When NOT to use it:** First-time blocks, body-related blocks, genuine task vagueness. The Dickens Process creates emotional intensity. Don't deploy it when the problem is simpler.

## 3. Paul Graham: Keep Your Identity Small

### The Essay

Graham's insight: "The more labels you have for yourself, the dumber they make you." When a topic is tied to your identity, you can't think clearly about it. You stop evaluating evidence and start defending your self-image.

Applied to the stuck person: **when a task is identity-charged, rational thinking about it becomes impossible.** They can't evaluate "is this good enough?" because the question is really "am I good enough?" They can't think about "how should I approach this?" because the question is really "am I the kind of person who can approach this at all?"

### Application

The intervention is to temporarily de-identify from the task.

- **"This isn't about whether you're a good [designer/writer/developer]. This is about putting shapes on a screen for 15 minutes."** Strip the identity charge. Make it small. Make it about the action, not what the action means.
- **"You're not launching your career. You're writing one paragraph."** Reduce the symbolic weight. The task is not a referendum on your worth. It is a physical action with a start and end.
- **"What if you did this as practice, not performance?"** Practice doesn't threaten identity. Performance does. Reframing the task as practice — "this is a draft," "this is an experiment," "this doesn't count yet" — removes the identity stakes temporarily.

**Coach line:** "You're treating this like a verdict on who you are. It's not. It's one task on one day. Lower the stakes in your head, then start."

## 4. Peter Thiel: The Contrarian Truth About Yourself

### Zero-to-One Identity Transition

> "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"

Applied to identity: **what truth about yourself are you afraid to act on?**

The person stuck on a Type E block often has a contrarian truth they haven't voiced:
- "I'm actually capable of more than anyone expects of me."
- "I belong in the room, even though I feel like I don't."
- "This task isn't above my level — my level is above where I've been performing."

They know this. They feel it. But acting on it would mean breaking from the consensus reality of their self-image — the image that was installed by past experiences, other people's opinions, and years of playing small.

### Definite Optimism vs. Definite Pessimism About Self

Thiel's framework of definite optimism (the future will be good and I will make it so) vs. definite pessimism (the future will be bad and I know why) applies to self-perception:

- **Definite optimism about self:** "I can do this. I will figure it out. The evidence supports it."
- **Definite pessimism about self:** "I can't do this. I know why. Here's my list of reasons."

The person with a Type E block is usually a definite pessimist about their own capability in this specific domain. They have a detailed, well-argued case for why they can't. The case feels rational. But it's built on the self-image thermostat, not on evidence.

**Coach line:** "You have a very detailed story about why you can't do this. What if the story is wrong? What if the person who told you that — including the version of you who decided it — was working with incomplete information?"

## 5. Steve Jobs: Refusing Installed Limitations

### "Stay Hungry, Stay Foolish"

Jobs' Stanford commencement speech is fundamentally about refusing the limitations others install in you. Getting fired from Apple — the company he founded — could have been a defining limitation. Instead: "The heaviness of being successful was replaced by the lightness of being a beginner again. It freed me to enter one of the most creative periods of my life."

The comeback narrative: Jobs' identity had to break before it could rebuild. The person who returned to Apple in 1997 was not the same person who was fired in 1985. The second version was more capable *because* the old identity shattered.

### The Reality Distortion Field Applied to Self

Jobs was famous for making engineers believe they could do things they thought impossible. He applied the same force to himself — refusing to accept that anything was beyond his capability, even when evidence suggested otherwise.

For the person with a Type E block: **the "evidence" that they can't do this is as reliable as the evidence that said the Mac couldn't be built in the timeline Jobs demanded.** Past failures, others' opinions, their own self-doubt — these are not evidence. These are the limitations they've accepted as fact.

**Coach line:** "You're carrying someone else's verdict about what you're capable of. Whose verdict is it? And why are you still obeying it?"

## 6. Neuroscience: Amygdala, Ego Threat, and the aMCC

### The Brain Doesn't Distinguish Physical Danger from Identity Threat

Neuroimaging research shows that social rejection and ego threat activate the same neural circuitry as physical pain — including the anterior insula and dorsal anterior cingulate cortex. The amygdala does not distinguish "a tiger is chasing me" from "this task might prove I'm not good enough."

When the person encounters a task that threatens their self-image, the amygdala fires a threat response. The body enters a mild (or not so mild) fight-flight-freeze state:
- Heart rate increases slightly
- Muscles tense (especially jaw, shoulders, gut — hence "I feel sick thinking about it")
- Prefrontal cortex partially shuts down (harder to think clearly, plan, or reason)
- The urge to escape intensifies (avoidance becomes almost irresistible)

This is why Type E feels different from other blocks. It's not flat (Type D). It's not scattered (Type B). It's **loaded** — tense, heavy, sometimes nauseating. The body is in threat mode.

### The Anterior Mid-Cingulate Cortex

Huberman's research on the aMCC is especially relevant here. The aMCC grows when you do things you don't want to do — specifically things that involve resistance and discomfort. For Type E, the discomfort is identity-level. Pushing through a Type E block is one of the highest-value aMCC exercises available.

**Framing for the person:** "Your brain is treating this task like a physical threat. It's not. It's an identity threat — and those feel the same to the amygdala. The resistance you're feeling right now? That's the exact signal that pushing through will grow the part of your brain that makes you stronger. This is the rep that counts."

## 7. Jordan Peterson: Voluntary Confrontation with Chaos

### Responsibility and Meaning

Peterson's core argument: "The more responsibility you take on, the more meaning your life has." Avoiding meaningful work is not a neutral choice — it is a choice that leads to depression, stagnation, and resentment.

The task the person is avoiding is almost always meaningful. That's WHY they're avoiding it. If it didn't matter, there would be no identity threat. The fact that it scares them is evidence that it matters.

**"You're not avoiding a task. You're avoiding growth. And avoiding growth is the most expensive thing you can do."**

### The Task as Dragon

Peterson frequently uses the mythological metaphor of the dragon guarding the treasure. The thing you most need is in the place you least want to look. The task that triggers the most avoidance is often the task that would create the most transformation if completed.

**"The task you're avoiding is the dragon. The thing you need — confidence, proof of capability, a new self-image — is behind it. You don't get the treasure by finding a different cave. You get it by going into this one."**

### When Avoidance Becomes Stagnation

Peterson's warning: chronic avoidance of meaningful challenge does not lead to comfort. It leads to depression. The person who consistently ducks Type E tasks does not become happier — they become smaller. Their world contracts. Their self-image, rather than being protected by the avoidance, actually degrades further.

This is the long-term cost that the Dickens Process makes visceral. "You're protecting yourself from failure. But the cost of that protection is that you never find out what you're capable of. And that's a worse failure than any task outcome."

## 8. Coaching Scripts

### Light Touch (first-time identity brush, mild resistance)

Use when: The task is new or high-visibility but the person is otherwise functional. First time hitting this wall. They can name the discomfort.

> "This one feels different from the others. Not vague, not overwhelming — just... heavy. Like something's at stake beyond the task itself."

> "What's the worst that happens if this isn't perfect? Say it out loud."

*Wait for answer. Usually something like "people will think I'm not good enough" or "I'll find out I can't actually do this."*

> "Got it. That's the identity piece — the task stopped being about the work and started being about what it means about you. Here's the thing: you don't need it to be perfect. You need it to exist. What's the version that's good enough for right now?"

Create the "good enough" micro-task. Schedule it now.

### Medium (pattern visible, recurring wall)

Use when: This is the 2nd or 3rd time this type of task has been avoided. The person recognizes the pattern but can't break it.

> "This is the same wall you hit on [previous similar task]. The task changes, but the block doesn't. That means the block isn't about the task — it's about something deeper."

> "Quick question: when did you first learn you couldn't do this kind of work? Not when did you first fail at it. When did you first *decide* you weren't the kind of person who does this?"

*Wait for the answer. Don't rush it. This is a dehypnotization moment.*

> "That person — the one who told you that, or the younger version of you who decided it — was working with incomplete information. They didn't know what you know now. They hadn't done what you've done since."

> "The self-image is a thermostat. It's set to a temperature from years ago. The only way to reset it is to do the thing the thermostat says you can't. Not perfectly. Just at all."

> "What's the smallest version of [TASK NAME] that contradicts the old story? Something that proves the thermostat is set wrong?"

Create the contradiction task. Schedule it now.

### Heavy (entrenched identity ceiling, 3rd+ time)

Use when: The person has hit this same type of block multiple times. Pattern interrupts don't work. State changes don't work. The block is deep and recurring.

> "Every time we get here — [describe the pattern: presenting work, shipping publicly, asking for money, etc.] — the same thing happens. You can do everything else. But this one category of work hits a wall."

> "That wall has a name. It's your self-image thermostat. It's set to 'I'm not the person who [does this thing].' And every time you approach the temperature above the thermostat, the mechanism kicks in and pulls you back."

> "I want you to feel something for a second. If this pattern continues for another 6 months — avoiding [this category of work] — what does that cost you? Not the task. Your life. Your trajectory. Feel that."

*10-second pause.*

> "Now: what opens up if you break through it today? Not the task. The pattern. What kind of person are you on the other side?"

*Wait for answer.*

> "That person — the one on the other side — what would they do right now? Not the big version. The smallest first move. What would they start with?"

Create that task. Schedule it now.

### Dickens-Level (deep identity work, rare — use only when nothing else has worked)

Use when: All other interventions have failed. This is the 4th+ occurrence. The person is aware of the block and still can't move. Recovery is green.

> "We've tried everything on this one. Pattern interrupts. Micro-tasks. State changes. And you're still here, stuck on the same wall. That tells me this isn't an execution problem. It's an identity problem."

> "Close your eyes for a second. I'm going to ask you three questions. Don't analyze — just feel the answers."

> "One: picture yourself 6 months from now, still avoiding this kind of work. What does your life look like? What have you missed? What do you feel about yourself? Hold that image."

*10-second pause.*

> "Two: picture yourself one year from now, same pattern. The things you wanted to build — did you build them? The person you wanted to become — are you them? Feel that."

*10-second pause.*

> "Three: now picture this. You start today. Not perfectly. Badly, even. But you start. And you keep starting. And six months from now, you've done the thing a dozen times. What's different? Who are you?"

*Wait for their response.*

> "The version of you in that third picture — they started exactly where you are right now. Same fear. Same thermostat. They just decided the cost of staying here was higher than the cost of being bad at it for a while."

> "What's the absolute smallest move that starts you toward that third picture? Not the whole task. One action. One thing that contradicts the old story."

## 9. The "Smallest Possible Version" Technique

### One Micro-Experience That Contradicts the Self-Image

The self-image thermostat doesn't reset through insight alone. It resets through *experience*. Specifically: through experiences that contradict the current self-image and are too real to dismiss.

The smallest possible version is designed to create exactly this kind of contradiction:

- If the self-image says "I'm not someone who ships things" -> ship one small thing. A tweet. A single page. A rough draft sent to one person.
- If the self-image says "I can't handle criticism" -> request feedback on one tiny piece from one trusted person.
- If the self-image says "I'm not good enough for this level" -> do 15 minutes of work at that level, with zero expectation of quality.

**The key:** the experience must be small enough that the thermostat doesn't trigger a full stress response, but real enough that the person can't dismiss it as "that doesn't count."

### Why It Works

Each micro-experience creates a small data point that the self-image must incorporate. "I did ship something." "I did handle feedback." "I did work at that level." Over time, these data points accumulate and the thermostat adjusts.

This is Maltz's dehypnotization in action — not through analysis of the past, but through creation of new experiences in the present.

**Coach line:** "You don't need to transform your self-image today. You need to create one experience that doesn't fit the old story. One. What would that be?"

## 10. ADHD-Specific Identity Wounds

### Years of "Not Living Up to Potential"

ADHD adults have heard some version of "you're so smart, why can't you just..." for their entire lives. This creates a specific identity wound: the belief that capability should automatically translate to output, and when it doesn't, something is fundamentally wrong with them.

This wound is especially damaging for Type E blocks because it adds a layer of shame on top of the identity threat: "Not only might I fail at this, but if I do, it proves that everyone who said I wasn't living up to my potential was right."

**The reframe:** "Your brain works differently. Not worse. Differently. The gap between your capability and your output isn't a character flaw — it's a wiring difference that requires different strategies, not more willpower."

### Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)

RSD is the extreme emotional sensitivity to perceived rejection or criticism that is common in ADHD. It is not a formal clinical diagnosis but describes a real neurological phenomenon: the ADHD brain processes rejection/criticism signals with significantly more intensity than neurotypical brains.

For Type E blocks, RSD means:
- The potential for criticism is experienced as potential for unbearable emotional pain.
- Even imagined criticism (before anything is published, shared, or submitted) triggers the avoidance response.
- The person may not be able to articulate the fear clearly because RSD operates at a pre-verbal, emotional level. They just know the task makes them feel terrible.

**Coaching adjustment:** Be especially careful about adding any pressure that could be perceived as criticism. "You should be able to do this" or "this isn't that hard" will trigger RSD and make the block worse. Instead: "This kind of task triggers a big emotional response for you. That's real. It's not weakness. But we still need to move. What's the version that feels safest to start with?"

### The Twice-Exceptional Trap

Many ADHD adults are "twice exceptional" — high intellectual capability combined with ADHD-related executive function challenges. This creates a unique identity prison:

- Too capable to be given accommodations or understanding ("You're so smart, you don't need help").
- Too inconsistent to trust their own output ("Sometimes I produce amazing work and sometimes I can't produce anything").
- The gap between what they know they *can* do and what they *actually* do becomes a source of profound shame.

For Type E blocks, the twice-exceptional person is not just afraid of failing. They are afraid of confirming the thing they most fear about themselves: that the inconsistency IS them, that the capability was an illusion, that they are fundamentally broken.

**Coach line:** "The fact that you can see the quality gap — that you know what great looks like and fear you can't produce it — is itself evidence of capability. People who can't do this kind of work don't lose sleep over whether they can do it. Your anxiety is proof of your potential, not disproof of it."

### Shame Spirals

ADHD emotional dysregulation can turn a Type E moment into a shame spiral:
1. Task triggers identity threat
2. Avoidance begins
3. Awareness of avoidance triggers shame ("I'm doing it again")
4. Shame intensifies the negative self-image
5. Intensified negative self-image makes the task feel even more threatening
6. More avoidance, more shame, repeat

**Interrupt the spiral, not just the avoidance.** Name the shame directly: "You're not just stuck. You're stuck AND beating yourself up for being stuck. That second part is making the first part worse. Separate them. Being stuck is a problem to solve. Shame about being stuck is noise. Drop the shame first, then we'll solve the stuck."

## 11. When to Escalate to /reflect

Type E blocks are the most likely to require deeper work beyond what /unblock can provide. Watch for these signals:

### Escalation Signals

- **Same block type, 4+ occurrences.** The pattern is entrenched beyond what a 5-minute intervention can address.
- **Emotional flooding.** The person becomes tearful, overwhelmed, or shuts down when the identity block is named. This needs more space than /unblock provides.
- **Formative story surfaces.** They name a specific event, person, or period that installed the limitation. This deserves full exploration, not a quick dehypnotization.
- **Months/years of the same avoidance.** If this block type has been running for a long time, the thermostat is deeply set. Resetting it requires sustained work.
- **The block is about more than work.** If the identity limitation shows up in relationships, health, and finances — not just this task — it needs a broader intervention.

### How to Escalate

Don't abandon the current session. Complete the micro-task first. THEN:

> "We got you moving today. But this pattern is bigger than one task. The same wall keeps appearing. `/reflect` is where we go deeper — not just 'what's blocking me right now' but 'where did this block come from and how do I actually reset the thermostat.' Next time you have 20 minutes and some emotional space, do a `/reflect` focused specifically on [the pattern you named]."

Create a Focuh task: "Reflect: [identity pattern]" scheduled for a day when they have space.

## 12. Misdiagnosis Signals

### Skill Gap Disguised as Identity Block

**Signal:** They describe the block in identity terms ("I'm not good enough") but when pressed, the real issue is that they genuinely don't know HOW to do the task.

- They've never done this type of work before.
- When you ask about specific steps, they can't describe them.
- The "fear" is actually "confusion" wearing a fear costume.

**Test:** "If someone showed you exactly how to do this — step by step — would the fear go away?" If yes, this is a skill gap, not an identity block. The intervention is learning, not dehypnotization. Help them find a tutorial, example, or mentor — then the block dissolves.

### Comfort Avoidance Disguised as Fear

**Signal:** They claim identity-level fear, but the task is not actually high-stakes.

- The task is routine, not visible or identity-defining.
- They describe "fear" but their body language says "don't wanna."
- The real issue is that the task is boring, uncomfortable, or tedious — not threatening.

**Test:** "On a scale of 1-10, how much does this task actually scare you vs. how much do you just not want to do it?" If the answer is low fear, high "don't wanna," this is Type C avoidance, not Type E identity. Switch to pattern interrupt.

### Perfectionism as Type E Costume

**Signal:** They frame the block as "I need it to be perfect" or "I'm afraid it won't be good enough."

This CAN be Type E (perfectionism rooted in identity — "my worth depends on the quality of my output"). But it can also be:

- **Type A in disguise:** The task is genuinely unclear and "not good enough" means "I don't know what good looks like yet." Intervention: define "done" more clearly.
- **Habit, not identity:** Some people have learned perfectionism as a work style, not an identity prison. They just need permission to ship rough. "Done is better than perfect. Ship the 80% version."

**Test:** "If I guaranteed that nobody would judge the quality — they'd only know whether you did it or not — would you start?" If yes: the block is about external judgment, which may be RSD rather than deep identity. If no: the block is about internal standards, which is more likely true Type E.

## 13. The One-Liner Summary

**The block isn't the task. It's the thermostat. Your self-image has a ceiling and the task is above it. You don't break through by trying harder — trying harder is purpose tremor. You break through by doing the smallest version that proves the ceiling is set wrong. One experience that contradicts the old story. That's how thermostats move.**
