# Type D: Low Energy / Can't Get Going

"I just can't." The intention is there. The knowledge is there. The body and mind won't engage. This is the block where waiting for motivation is the trap — because motivation is not coming.

---

## 1. Huberman's Dopamine System — The Deep Mechanics

### Baseline Dopamine vs. Peaks and Troughs

Dopamine is not a "reward chemical." It is a motivation and movement chemical. It drives the desire to pursue, not the pleasure of having. Understanding the pool/wave dynamics is essential for coaching this block type.

**The dopamine wave pool** (Huberman's analogy):

- **Baseline:** The standing level of dopamine in the brain. This determines your general sense of motivation, energy, and willingness to engage. Everyone has a different baseline.
- **Peaks:** Spikes above baseline caused by exciting, rewarding, or novel experiences. The higher the peak, the more motivated and energized you feel.
- **Troughs:** Drops BELOW baseline that follow peaks. The trough depth is proportional to the peak height. After a big dopamine spike (sugar, social media binge, video game session, exciting news), the trough is deep.

**The trough is what Type D feels like.** Low motivation. Flat affect. Staring at the screen. Can't start. The person is sitting in a dopamine trough — their level is below their own baseline. No amount of "wanting to work" will override this neurochemistry.

### Why Waiting for Motivation Does Not Work

This is the single most important principle for Type D:

**Motivation follows action. Action does not follow motivation.**

The person in a trough is waiting to "feel like" starting. That feeling IS dopamine. They are waiting for the thing they are deficient in. It's like waiting to feel hydrated before drinking water.

The only exit from a trough is action that generates dopamine through a different pathway — physical movement, cold exposure, or direct engagement with the task (the 2-minute bridge). Waiting deepens the trough as the brain continues to seek easy relief (phone scrolling, snacking) which produces small peaks followed by even deeper troughs.

### Limbic Friction

Huberman defines "limbic friction" as the effort required to overcome your current autonomic state to engage in a desired behavior. It comes in two flavors:

**Low-energy limbic friction (Type D primary):**
- The autonomic nervous system is tilted toward parasympathetic (rest/digest).
- The person needs to energize UP to engage.
- Intervention: stimulating — cold exposure, exercise, movement, bright light.

**Anxious limbic friction (Type D secondary, or Type E overlap):**
- The autonomic nervous system is tilted toward sympathetic (fight/flight).
- The person needs to calm DOWN to engage.
- Intervention: calming — breathing exercises, meditation, reducing stimulation.

**Diagnosis matters.** If someone says "I can't get going" and they're anxious/wired but frozen, they need calming, not stimulation. If they're genuinely sluggish and flat, they need activation. Giving activation to an anxious person makes it worse. Giving calming to a sluggish person makes them sleepier.

**Quick diagnostic:** "Are you feeling flat and sluggish, or wired but stuck?" This determines the intervention direction.

## 2. The Physiology-First Principle

### Tony Robbins' Triad — Body Leads, Brain Follows

"You don't change your mind first, then move. You move first, and your mind follows."

This is the core operating principle for Type D. The person is trying to think their way into action. They are trying to find the motivation, the reason, the willpower. All of that is cognitive. And cognition is downstream of physiology.

The Triad order for Type D:

1. **Physiology** (change the body state FIRST)
2. **Focus** (once the body is activated, direct attention to one specific thing)
3. **Language** (reframe from "I can't" to "what's the smallest version")

### Specific Physical Interventions and Their Neurochemistry

**Cold water on the face (5-15 seconds):**
- Activates the mammalian dive reflex via the trigeminal nerve.
- Heart rate drops ~22% on average (bradycardia onset at ~5.6 seconds, peak at ~35.8 seconds).
- Paradoxically, while heart rate drops (parasympathetic), alertness INCREASES. The dive reflex redistributes blood to the brain and core.
- Net effect: calm alertness — the ideal state for task initiation.
- This is the single fastest non-pharmacological state change available. Takes 5-15 seconds. No equipment needed.

**Short burst exercise (2-10 minutes):**
- Even 10 minutes of brisk walking increases dopamine and norepinephrine.
- Norepinephrine increases are brain-region specific, with increases in the cortex and striatum (areas responsible for attention and goal-directed behavior).
- The spike begins within minutes of starting. Doesn't require a full workout.
- 20 pushups, 10 burpees, jumping jacks, or a fast walk around the block is sufficient.
- These produce a dopamine and norepinephrine increase that lasts significantly longer than the exercise itself — hours, not minutes.

**Posture change (immediate):**
- Research on embodied cognition shows that upright posture increases positive mood states, self-esteem, and willingness to engage with challenge.
- Sitting upright makes it easier to recall positive memories; slouching increases negative and depressive thoughts.
- Standing up from a seated position changes blood pressure distribution and increases cortical arousal.
- The postural shift is especially important because Type D often presents with a collapsed, slumped physical state that both reflects and reinforces the low-energy condition.

**Bright light exposure:**
- Bright light (especially sunlight) within 30-60 minutes of waking increases cortisol pulse and dopamine.
- If the person is in a dim room, opening blinds or stepping outside for 2-5 minutes can shift the neurochemical landscape.

### The Sequence

Physical interventions should be stacked, not picked individually:

```
1. Stand up (posture change -- 5 seconds)
2. Cold water on face (dive reflex -- 15 seconds)
3. 20 pushups or 2-minute walk (exercise spike -- 2 minutes)
4. Sit down in a different spot or with changed environment (context shift)
5. Begin the micro-task
```

Total time: 3-5 minutes. Total neurochemical shift: significant increase in dopamine, norepinephrine, and cortical arousal.

## 3. The Difference Between Tired and Unmotivated

This is the most important diagnostic distinction in Type D. Getting it wrong means either pushing an exhausted person (harmful) or validating a dopamine trough with rest (counterproductive).

### Physiological Fatigue (Actually Tired)

**Signals from data:**
- WHOOP recovery < 33% (red)
- Sleep < 6 hours or sleep efficiency < 75%
- HRV significantly below personal baseline
- Declining HRV trend over multiple days
- High strain previous day without adequate recovery

**Behavioral signals:**
- Tired across ALL tasks, not task-specific
- Physical symptoms: heavy eyes, yawning, body aches
- Was active/productive yesterday but crashed today
- Recently ill, traveling, or under extreme stress

**Correct response:** Do NOT push through. Genuine exhaustion depletes the neurochemical resources needed for self-regulation. Low HRV = depleted willpower at the physiological level.

> "Your recovery is [X]%. You slept [Y] hours. You don't have a motivation problem — you have an energy problem. Pushing through now will make tomorrow worse. What's the absolute minimum? 15 minutes of your most important task, then actually rest. Not 'try to rest while feeling guilty.' Done for real."

### Dopamine Trough (Unmotivated But Not Exhausted)

**Signals from data:**
- WHOOP recovery > 66% (green) or 33-66% (yellow)
- Adequate sleep (7+ hours)
- HRV at or above baseline
- No recent physical overexertion

**Behavioral signals:**
- "I can't get going" but could scroll phone, watch YouTube, eat snacks
- Task-general malaise, not task-specific dread
- Flat, not anxious
- "Waiting to feel like it"
- Have been in this state for 1-3 hours with no change

**Correct response:** This is a dopamine problem, not a rest problem. Resting will not fix it — resting IS the trough. Physical state change is the intervention.

> "Your body is recovered — recovery is green, you slept well. This isn't tiredness. This is a dopamine trough. Your brain is in low gear and waiting won't fix it. The trough only deepens if you sit here. Time to change your state."

### The WHOOP Cross-Reference Decision Matrix

| Recovery | Sleep | HRV Trend | Diagnosis | Intervention |
|----------|-------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Red (<33%) | <6 hrs | Declining | Physiological fatigue | Minimum viable task, then rest. Move tasks to tomorrow. |
| Red (<33%) | 7+ hrs | Stable | Possible illness/overtraining | Gentle inquiry. Micro-task only if they want to. |
| Yellow (33-66%) | <6 hrs | Declining | Moderate fatigue | Reduced ambition. 1-2 priority tasks only. Physiology-first. |
| Yellow (33-66%) | 7+ hrs | Stable | Mild fatigue + dopamine issue | State change, then micro-task. Don't push for full day. |
| Green (66%+) | 7+ hrs | Stable/Rising | Dopamine trough -- not tired | Full state change protocol. No excuses. Push. |
| Green (66%+) | 7+ hrs | Stable | But avoiding ONE specific task | Probably Type C, not Type D. Re-diagnose. |

## 4. "Steepen the Trough" — Huberman's Counter-Intuitive Protocol

### The Concept

When you're in a dopamine trough, the intuitive response is to reach for something easy and pleasant to "get going." Check phone. Eat something sweet. Watch a quick video. Scroll social media.

This is the worst possible move. Each easy dopamine hit produces a micro-peak followed by a proportional drop. The trough gets shallower momentarily, then deeper. The person ends up further below baseline than when they started.

Huberman's protocol: **do something HARDER and more uncomfortable than the current state.** This steepens the trough — makes it temporarily worse — which triggers a stronger dopamine rebound that brings you back to (or above) baseline faster.

### Specific Protocols

**Cold exposure (primary recommendation):**
- Cold shower for 30-60 seconds, or cold water on face for 15 seconds.
- Deliberate cold exposure increases dopamine 250-500% above baseline.
- The increase is sustained — lasting for hours, not minutes.
- The discomfort itself is the mechanism. The brain's response to deliberate cold exposure is a robust catecholamine release (dopamine + norepinephrine + epinephrine).

**Brief intense exercise:**
- Not a gentle walk. Pushups to near-failure. Burpees. Sprint in place for 30 seconds.
- The intensity matters. Easy exercise provides a small bump. Intense brief exercise provides a large spike.
- 2-5 minutes is sufficient.

**The "cringe test":**
- If the intervention makes you cringe thinking about doing it, it's the right tool.
- The cringe = the limbic friction = the barrier that generates the rebound.
- "If it doesn't suck, it won't work for this."

### What NOT to Do in a Trough

| Easy Dopamine Source | Why It Fails |
|---------------------|-------------|
| Phone/social media scrolling | Micro-peaks followed by deeper troughs. Variable reward schedule is addictive and depleting. |
| Sugar/junk food | Glucose spike then insulin crash then deeper fatigue. |
| Caffeine (excessive) | Blocks adenosine but doesn't create dopamine. Masks fatigue without resolving it. Creates anxiety-type limbic friction. |
| "Just one episode" | 45 minutes of passive entertainment deepens the parasympathetic tilt. |
| Napping (if not genuinely exhausted) | Sleeping in a trough just extends the trough. Only appropriate for genuine fatigue. |

## 5. ADHD-Specific Dopamine Deficits

### The Baseline Problem

ADHD brains have lower baseline dopamine activity and less consistent dopamine signaling in the circuits responsible for attention and goal-directed behavior. This means:

- The trough is **deeper** relative to baseline. A neurotypical trough might be a 20% drop; an ADHD trough might feel like a 40% drop.
- The **threshold for task initiation is higher.** Tasks that lack novelty, urgency, or immediate payoff often fail to generate enough activation to trigger the "go" signal.
- **Recovery from troughs is slower** without intervention. The ADHD brain takes longer to return to baseline naturally.

### Why Stimulant Medication Works (and Its Limits)

Stimulant medication (methylphenidate/Ritalin, amphetamine/Adderall) works by raising baseline dopamine and norepinephrine. This does three things:

1. Makes the trough shallower (starting from a higher baseline, the drop is less severe).
2. Lowers the task initiation threshold (less activation needed to "go").
3. Improves sustained attention (more consistent dopamine signaling).

**But medication does not:**
- Create meaning or motivation for tasks that are genuinely misaligned.
- Resolve emotional avoidance or identity blocks (Type E).
- Fix the trough caused by overuse of easy dopamine sources (phone, sugar, etc.).
- Replace physical state change when the trough is deep.

For unmedicated individuals or during medication gaps (weekends, evenings, wearing off): physical state change is the most reliable non-pharmacological intervention. It is not as powerful as medication but it is available immediately and consistently.

### The Emotional Component

Over time, tasks become emotionally charged for ADHD adults. Years of criticism, internalized shame, and failed starts create emotional associations with task initiation itself. When this emotional load is high, the brain avoids initiation as self-protection.

Signal: "I know I should just start but my whole body resists it." This is not laziness and it is not a pure dopamine deficit — it is learned emotional avoidance layered on top of neurochemical deficit. The physical state change addresses the neurochemistry. The emotional component may need Type E intervention.

## 6. Steve Jobs — The Meaning Check

### "If today were the last day of my life..."

Jobs' daily practice for 33 years: "I have looked in the mirror every morning and asked myself: 'If today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I am about to do today?' And whenever the answer has been 'no' for too many days in a row, I know I need to change something."

### Application to Type D

When someone "can't get going," there are two possibilities:

1. **They can't get going on ANYTHING.** This is a state problem — dopamine trough, fatigue, physiological. Intervention: state change.
2. **They can't get going on THIS TASK specifically.** This might be a meaning problem, not an energy problem.

The Jobs test: "If this were your last day, would this task make the cut? If the answer is no — not today, not 'eventually,' but it genuinely doesn't matter to you — maybe the block isn't energy. Maybe the task is wrong."

This is a delicate coaching move. Use it only when:
- Recovery is green and sleep is good (ruling out physiology).
- The state change has been attempted and failed.
- The person has energy for other things but specifically cannot engage this task.
- The task has been chronically avoided, not just today.

If those conditions are met:

> "You're rested. Your body is ready. We've tried the state change. But you still can't move on this. Let me ask you something honestly: does this task actually matter to you? Not to your client. Not to the deadline. To you. Because if the answer is no too many days in a row, maybe the block is telling you something."

**IMPORTANT:** This is NOT license to abandon every hard task. Most of the time, Type D is genuine dopamine trough and the intervention is physical. The Jobs meaning-check is a last resort when all physical interventions have failed and the block appears task-specific rather than general.

## 7. Jordan Peterson: The Smallest Bearable Step

### "What's the smallest thing you could do that you would actually do?"

Peterson's question cuts through the ambition trap. When energy is low, the brain rejects any task that feels like "effort." But it will accept something small enough to be bearable.

The key word is *actually*. Not "should do." Not "could do if I tried." Would *actually* do, right now, in this state. The answer might be embarrassingly small: "Open the file." "Write one sentence." "Read the first paragraph." That's fine. The goal is not the action itself — it's the momentum it creates.

### Meaning Deficit

Peterson's deeper framework: if you can't get going, the task may not connect to anything that matters. Meaningful work generates its own energy. Meaningless work drains it.

- If the task connects to something they care about but they still can't start: it's a state problem (dopamine/energy). Solve with physiology.
- If the task genuinely doesn't connect to anything they care about: it's a meaning problem. Either find the connection or question whether the task belongs on the list at all.

### Order-Chaos Balance

Too much chaos (scattered, no plan, no structure) = anxiety and paralysis.
Too much order (rigid schedule, over-planned, everything mapped) = stagnation and rebellion.

Type D sometimes appears when the person's day is over-structured. The brain is rebelling against too much imposed order. Signal: they have a detailed plan and zero desire to follow it. The intervention may be to *loosen* the plan, not tighten it: "Which part of this plan actually interests you? Start there. Let the rest flex."

## 8. Coaching Scripts

### Light — Body Is Actually Tired (Red/Yellow Recovery)

> "Recovery at [X]%. You slept [Y] hours. Your body is running on empty."
>
> "Here's the deal: I'm not going to ask you to power through. That's a losing bet today. But I'm also not going to let you spiral into 'the whole day is lost.'"
>
> "What's the absolute minimum version of [TASK NAME]? The tiniest piece that would let you close the laptop feeling like you moved it forward. 15 minutes. Maybe less."
>
> "After that — done for real. Not 'I'll try to rest.' Done. Put the laptop away. Go outside. Nap. Whatever recovery looks like. But first: 15 minutes on the one thing that matters."

Create a 15-minute micro-task. Move remaining tasks to tomorrow. Schedule the micro-task now.

### Medium — Body Is Fine, Dopamine Trough (Green Recovery)

> "Your recovery is [X]% — body is ready. You slept [Y] hours. This isn't tiredness. This is a dopamine trough."
>
> "Your brain is in low gear and waiting isn't going to fix it. Every minute you sit here 'trying to get going' is a minute the trough deepens. Scrolling your phone, checking email — that makes it worse, not better."
>
> "Here's what works: make it harder, not easier. Cold water on your face — 15 seconds. Or 20 pushups right now. Walk outside for 3 minutes. Pick one. Do it before you respond to me."
>
> [Wait for them to do it]
>
> "Good. Now: [TASK NAME]. Not the whole thing. One specific piece. What's the first 15 minutes look like?"
>
> [They describe it]
>
> "That's the task. Starting in 10 minutes — give the state change time to hit. I'm scheduling it now."

Create a 15-minute micro-task. Schedule it 10-15 minutes from now. Be specific in the task title.

### Heavy — Extended Staring, Deep Trough

> "You've been staring at the screen for [X amount of time]. Every minute you sit here not starting, the trough gets deeper. Your brain is telling you to wait for motivation. Motivation isn't coming. Motivation is dopamine. You're in a dopamine deficit. You cannot wait for the thing you're deficient in."
>
> "You have two options right now. Option one: make it harder. Cold shower. 30 seconds. Not pleasant — that's the point. The discomfort triggers a dopamine rebound that lasts for hours. Or: 2 minutes of intense exercise — pushups to failure, burpees, sprint in place."
>
> "Option two: surrender honestly. If your body truly cannot today — and sometimes it can't — then shut the laptop. Go rest. But ACTUALLY rest. Don't sit here in limbo doing nothing and calling it rest. Limbo is the worst of both worlds."
>
> "Which one? Make it harder, or surrender honestly? There is no option three."

If they choose to push: create micro-task, schedule 10 minutes out, full state change protocol first.
If they choose to surrender: move tasks to tomorrow, close the session cleanly. "Good. Rest well. Tomorrow we go."

## 9. The Scheduling Gap

### Why "Start Now" Doesn't Work for Type D

After a physical state change (cold water, exercise), the neurochemical shift takes 2-5 minutes to peak. Scheduling the task for "right now" means they're sitting down to work before the dopamine has arrived.

**The protocol:**

1. Physical state change (2-3 minutes)
2. Brief transition activity — get water, set up workspace, open the file (3-5 minutes)
3. Begin the micro-task (10-15 minutes after the state change)

Schedule the Focuh task for 10-15 minutes from now, not for right now. This gives the neurochemistry time to shift and prevents the discouraging experience of "I did the pushups and I still don't feel like it."

### The Peak Window

The dopamine/norepinephrine spike from cold exposure or intense exercise peaks at approximately 5-15 minutes and remains elevated for 1-3 hours. The micro-task should be scheduled within this window.

If the task is important and they have energy after the micro-task, they'll naturally continue (the 80% rule). If not, at least one focused block was captured during the peak window.

## 10. When to Surrender vs. Push Through

### The Decision Framework

| Condition | Decision | Action |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Red recovery + poor sleep (<6 hrs) | **Surrender** | Move tasks to tomorrow. 15-minute minimum version only if they want to. No guilt. |
| Red recovery + OK sleep (7+ hrs) | **Gentle push** | Something may be off (illness, overtraining). Micro-task only. Listen to the body. |
| Yellow recovery + poor sleep | **Reduced ambition** | 1-2 priority tasks only. State change first. Don't plan a full day. |
| Yellow recovery + OK sleep | **Push with support** | State change + micro-task. Aim for 1-2 hours of focused work, not a full day. |
| Green recovery + OK sleep | **Push** | Full state change protocol. This is a dopamine trough, not fatigue. No negotiation. |
| Green recovery + "can't get going" on one specific task | **Re-diagnose** | This is probably Type C (avoidance) or Type E (identity). Not Type D. |

### Surrendering Gracefully

When surrender is the right call, it must be clean:

> "Today isn't the day. Your body needs recovery and pushing through will make tomorrow worse. Here's what we're doing: I'm moving [tasks] to tomorrow. You're going to [specific rest activity — nap, walk outside, early bedtime]. Not 'productive rest' — actual rest. Close the laptop."

Do not leave them in limbo. Either they are working (with specific task and time) or they are resting (with laptop closed and tasks moved). The worst outcome is "trying to work but not really working" — that is neither productive nor restful and extends the trough.

## 11. Misdiagnosis Signals

### When "Low Energy" Is Actually Type C (Avoidance)

**The key test:** Do they have energy for OTHER things?

- They "can't get going" on the project but just spent 30 minutes organizing files.
- They feel "sluggish" about the pitch deck but had energy to answer all their emails.
- They "have no motivation" but just went on a long walk / cleaned the kitchen / did laundry.

This is not low energy. This is selective avoidance disguised as low energy. The energy exists — it's being directed away from the threatening task.

**Re-diagnosis probe:** "You said you can't get going. But you [did other things]. You have energy — just not for this. What's different about [the avoided task]?"

If the answer reveals discomfort, stress, or avoidance of the specific task: re-classify as Type C and switch to avoidance intervention.

### When "Low Energy" Is Actually Type E (Fear/Identity)

**The key test:** Does the task itself create dread, not just low motivation?

- "I feel sick thinking about it" (emotional, not energetic).
- The task involves visibility, judgment, or personal exposure.
- They've avoided this SPECIFIC task across multiple sessions.
- Energy returns when the task is removed from the plan ("Oh, I could do [other thing] instead!").

This is not a dopamine trough. This is emotional avoidance with a fear or identity root. The person doesn't need a cold shower — they need the identity intervention from Type E.

**Re-diagnosis probe:** "If I took [TASK NAME] off your plate entirely — replaced it with something else of equal difficulty — would you still feel this way? Or would the energy come back?"

If the energy would come back: this is task-specific, not state-general. Re-classify as Type E.

### The Energy vs. Motivation vs. Avoidance Diagnostic

| Question | Type D (Energy) | Type C (Avoidance) | Type E (Fear) |
|----------|----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Energy for other tasks? | No -- flat across everything | Yes -- busy with other things | Yes -- but not THIS task |
| WHOOP recovery? | Often red/yellow | Often green | Often green |
| Physical state? | Sluggish, heavy, flat | Normal or even energized | Tense, anxious, dread |
| What helps? | Physical state change | Pattern interrupt + 2-min bridge | Identity work, name the fear |
| What they say | "I just can't" | "I've been busy with other stuff" | "I feel sick thinking about it" |

## Quick Reference — Intervention Sequence for Type D

```
1. CHECK BODY       -> WHOOP recovery, sleep, HRV. Is this fatigue or a trough?

2a. IF FATIGUED:
   -> Validate: "Your body needs rest. This isn't a motivation problem."
   -> Minimum viable: 15-minute micro-task if they want to.
   -> Move remaining tasks to tomorrow.
   -> Close: "Rest. Actually rest. Tomorrow we go."

2b. IF TROUGH (body is fine):
   -> Name it: "This is a dopamine trough. Waiting won't fix it."
   -> State change: Cold water on face + 20 pushups + change location.
   -> Wait: 10 minutes for neurochemistry to shift.
   -> Micro-task: Schedule specific 15-minute action.
   -> Close: "Go. 15 minutes. The state change will carry you."

3. IF STILL STUCK after state change:
   -> Re-diagnose: "You changed your state and still can't start.
      Is this about energy, or is it about this specific task?"
   -> If task-specific: pivot to Type C or Type E.
   -> If general: consider genuine illness, medication timing,
      or burnout. Surrender may be correct.
```

Time from opening to action: under 5 minutes.
