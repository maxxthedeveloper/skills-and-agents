# Type A: Task Too Big / Vague

> "I don't know where to start."

The task is a project masquerading as an action. There is no clear first step. The servo-mechanism has no target to lock onto — so it idles, and the person feels "stuck" or "lazy" when in fact their brain is functioning exactly as designed.

---

## 1. Why This Happens: The Neuroscience

### The Dopamine Gate

Task initiation is not willpower. It is a neurochemical event. The prefrontal cortex evaluates a goal, sequences steps, and sends a "go" signal to the basal ganglia and motor circuits. Dopamine is the messenger that makes the brain register a task as *worth starting now*.

In ADHD brains, this system is structurally different:

- **Dopamine signaling is inconsistent.** Genetic variants in the DRD4 receptor and DAT1 transporter mean the prefrontal cortex gets weaker, noisier signals. The threshold to fire the "go" signal is higher.
- **ADHD brains require 2-3x more dopamine stimulation to initiate a task** compared to neurotypical brains. This is not a character deficit. It is receptor density.
- **Vague tasks produce almost zero dopamine.** Dopamine is released in anticipation of a *clear, specific reward*. "Work on the website" generates nothing. "Write the hero section headline" generates just enough.
- **Every embedded decision drains the launch battery.** A vague task front-loads dozens of micro-decisions (what first? which part? what tool? what standard?). Each decision draws on the same limited executive function resources that ADHD brains already have less of. The task becomes a wall of invisible choices, and initiation fails before it begins.

### Working Memory Bottleneck

Working memory — the mental scratchpad — is consistently impaired in ADHD. Research shows hypo-activation in the prefrontal cortex and cerebellum under higher cognitive load. The practical consequence:

- **Neurotypical working memory holds ~4 chunks. ADHD working memory often holds 2-3.** A 10-step project overflows the buffer instantly.
- **If you can't hold the full shape of a task in mind, you can't sequence it.** And if you can't sequence it, you can't start it.
- **Chunking into 3 groups is not a preference — it is a cognitive ceiling.** This is why "break it into smaller pieces" works, but only if the pieces number 3 or fewer.

### The Servo-Mechanism Needs a Target

Maxwell Maltz's core insight: the human mind operates like a torpedo's guidance system. It will self-correct toward any target that is clearly defined. But it *cannot engage without a target*. A vague task is like telling the torpedo "go somewhere in the ocean." The mechanism spins, burns fuel, and goes nowhere. This is not laziness. This is an unlocked guidance system.

**The fix is never "try harder." The fix is "make the target specific enough that the mechanism locks on automatically."**

## 2. Steve Jobs: Simplify Ruthlessly

> "Simple can be harder than complex. You have to work hard to get your thinking clean to make it simple. But it's worth it in the end, because once you get there, you can move mountains."

### The Principle

Jobs did not succeed by doing more. He succeeded by doing *radically less*, with *radical clarity*. When he returned to Apple in 1997, the company had dozens of products. He drew a 2x2 grid on a whiteboard: Consumer/Pro on one axis, Desktop/Portable on the other. Four products. Everything else was killed.

**He did not prioritize. He eliminated.**

### Application to a Stuck Task

When a task feels too big, the Jobs question is not "what's the first step?" It is:

- **"What can I remove?"** Most of the overwhelm comes from things that don't need to be there — edge cases, polish, features nobody asked for, standards you imposed on yourself.
- **"What is the simplest version that would actually matter?"** Not the MVP. The *minimum meaningful action*.
- **"What am I saying no to?"** Because if you haven't said no to anything, you haven't actually defined the task. You are carrying the ghost of the whole project.

**Coach line:** "You're not stuck because the task is too big. You're stuck because you haven't decided what to leave out. What can you cut?"

## 3. Paul Graham: Schlep Blindness and Smallest Concrete Version

### Schlep Blindness

> "The most dangerous thing about our dislike of schleps is that much of it is unconscious. Your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps. That's schlep blindness."

A "schlep" is a tedious, unpleasant task. Graham's insight: when someone says "I don't know where to start," they often *do* know — but the first step is unglamorous, uncomfortable, or boring. Their brain literally edits it out of awareness.

**In ADHD brains, schlep blindness is amplified.** The interest-based nervous system actively suppresses tasks that are boring, unclear, or emotionally uncomfortable. The person genuinely cannot "see" the obvious next step because it fails the novelty/urgency/interest filter.

**Application:** Ask: "Is there a step you already know you should do, that you're kind of pretending you don't see?" This cuts through the blindness directly.

### Do Things That Don't Scale

Graham's second relevant framework: start with the smallest, most manual, most un-scalable version.

- Don't build the system. Do the thing by hand once.
- Don't design the page. Write the words in a text file.
- Don't architect the API. Make one endpoint that does one thing.

**The smallest concrete version is always a task, never a project.** When someone converts their project into the smallest embarrassingly manual version, they have a task their brain can grab.

**Coach line:** "Forget the elegant version. What's the ugly, manual, embarrassing version you could do in 15 minutes? Do that."

## 4. Peter Thiel: Zero-to-One Thinking

> "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"

### The Power Law Applied to Tasks

Thiel's core insight in Zero to One: we don't live in a normal distribution. We live under a power law. One thing matters 10x more than everything else combined.

Applied to a stuck task:

- **Not all parts of the task are equal.** One sub-task will unlock 80% of the value. The rest is finishing work.
- **"What's the one action that would make the biggest dent?"** Not the first step chronologically. The highest-leverage step.
- **Don't optimize the plan. Find the singular move.** A person staring at a big task is often trying to figure out the *whole sequence*. Thiel's lens says: forget the sequence. Find the one thing that changes the game, and do only that.

### A Definite Person

> "A definite person determines the one best thing to do and then does it."

Indefinite thinking — "I'll figure it out as I go" — is what makes a task feel vague. Definite thinking — "the one thing I'm doing right now is X" — is what makes a task feel doable.

**Coach line:** "If you could only do ONE thing on this project today — and it had to create the most progress possible — what would it be? That's your task. Everything else is noise."

## 5. Tony Robbins RPM: Making the Target Vivid

### Why RPM Works for ADHD

The RPM framework solves the exact problem ADHD brains have with vague tasks: it creates emotional stakes and vivid imagery *before* asking for action. This generates the dopamine that ADHD brains need to initiate.

### The Three Questions (in order — sequence matters)

**R — Result: "What does DONE look like?"**
Not a list of deliverables. A single vivid image.
- Bad: "Finish the landing page."
- Good: "A live page that loads in under 2 seconds with a headline that makes me proud."
- ADHD key: The more *sensory and specific* the image, the more dopamine it generates. "See" the finished thing. If they can't see it, they can't start it.

**P — Purpose: "Why does this matter to YOU?"**
Not the deadline. Not the client. Not the obligation. The *emotional* why.
- Bad: "Because it's due Friday."
- Good: "Because I want to prove I can ship something beautiful on my own."
- ADHD key: Obligation-based motivation ("I should") produces cortisol. Desire-based motivation ("I want this") produces dopamine. Find the want.

**M — Massive Action Plan: "What are the 3 chunks?"**
Three. Not five. Not ten. Three groups that each feel like a complete unit of progress.
- Chunk 1 should be completable in 15-30 minutes.
- Each chunk has a clear "done" signal — a file saved, a section written, a component deployed.
- The first chunk is the one that gets scheduled NOW.

### The Chunking Rule of 3

This is not arbitrary. Working memory in ADHD maxes out at about 3 meaningful groups. Breaking a task into 10 pieces is just a different kind of overwhelm. Three chunks, each with a name and a done-state, is the maximum the mechanism can hold.

## 6. Andrew Huberman: The Visual Lock Protocol

Before diving into the task, use the visual focus protocol to prime the attentional system:

1. Pick a single point in your physical environment — a spot on the wall, a word on the screen.
2. Stare at it for 30-60 seconds. Breathe normally.
3. This narrowing of visual focus releases norepinephrine and dopamine, engaging the prefrontal circuits needed for sustained attention.
4. The subsequent work feels *less effortful* because the focus circuits are already warmed up.

**For Type A blocks specifically:** After the visual lock, state the ONE first action out loud. The combination of primed attention + verbal declaration creates enough activation energy to start.

## 7. Coaching Scripts

### Light Touch (task is genuinely unclear, first time stuck)

> "What does done look like? One sentence. Not the steps — the finished thing."

*Wait for answer. Then:*

> "Good. Now what's the first physical action — something you could do in the next 15 minutes that moves it forward?"

*If they give something vague like "research" or "figure out":*

> "That's still a project. What's the actual action? Open a file? Write a sentence? Send one message? Make it so small your brain can't argue with it."

### Medium (they know it's too big, need the reframe)

> "You're looking at a project and calling it a task. That's why your brain won't start. Projects don't have a 'start' — tasks do. Let's find the actual task hiding inside this."

> "Three questions, quick:
> 1. What does DONE look like — one sentence.
> 2. Why does this matter to you — not the deadline, to YOU.
> 3. What are the 3 chunks? Just names, not plans."

*After they answer:*

> "Chunk 1. How long?"
> *[They answer.]*
> "That's your next 15-30 minutes. Everything else does not exist right now."

### Heavy (recurring block, they've been stuck for days)

> "You've been staring at this for [X days]. Your brain isn't broken — the target is too vague for the mechanism to engage. A torpedo can't hit 'somewhere in the ocean.' It needs coordinates."

> "We're going to make this so clear your brain has no choice but to lock on. Tell me: if you did this perfectly, what does the finished thing look like? Be specific — what am I looking at?"

*After they describe it:*

> "That's the target. Now — what's the ONE sub-task that would create the most progress? Not the first step. The highest-leverage step. The one that makes everything else easier."

*Lock it in. Schedule it. Then:*

> "15 minutes. This one piece. Start now. The mechanism will take over once it has a target."

### Schlep-Bust Script (when they "don't know" but probably do)

> "I think you do know the first step. You just don't want to do it because it's boring or uncomfortable. Is there a step you can see that you've been pretending you can't?"

*If they identify one:*

> "That's the schlep. Your brain hid it from you because it's unglamorous. But that boring thing IS the work. 15 minutes on the schlep. Go."

## 8. ADHD-Specific Tactical Angles

### Time Blindness

"By Friday" is not a real deadline for an ADHD brain. Friday does not exist until it is Thursday night. The only time that exists is *right now* and *not now*.

- **Never use future deadlines as motivation.** They produce zero urgency until panic range.
- **Always anchor to the present:** "The next 15 minutes" is real. "Before lunch" is real. "By Friday" is fiction.
- **Schedule the micro-task for NOW, with a concrete end time.** "10:15 to 10:30, write the intro paragraph." Time-boxed present tense is the only tense that works.

### Novelty Deficit

If the task is boring, the ADHD brain will not start it regardless of importance. The interest-based nervous system runs on novelty, urgency, challenge, or personal interest — never on importance alone.

**Reframing tactics:**
- **Add a constraint:** "Write the headline in exactly 6 words." Constraints create challenge, challenge creates interest.
- **Change the medium:** "Don't type it. Say it out loud and record it." Novel format = novel dopamine.
- **Race the clock:** "Can you get this to 80% in 12 minutes?" Urgency is a dopamine trigger.
- **Make it a game:** "Write the worst possible version first." Lowering the stakes while adding play activates interest.

### Working Memory Limits

- **Never give more than 3 things at once.** Not 3 tasks + context + caveats. Three items, total.
- **Write it down, always.** Working memory cannot be relied on. The micro-task should be visible — in Focuh, on a sticky note, on the screen.
- **One instruction at a time.** After diagnosing, give ONE action. Not "here's your plan." One thing. Then the next.

## 9. Misdiagnosis Signals

Type A ("too big/vague") is the most commonly *claimed* block type because it is the least threatening to say. Watch for these signals that the real block is elsewhere:

### Actually Type E (Identity/Fear)

- They can describe the task clearly when pressed — it is not actually vague.
- The task involves visibility (publishing, presenting, launching) or judgment.
- They have broken this same task down before and still didn't start.
- Language: "I don't know if it's good enough" or "what if it doesn't work."
- **Test:** "If nobody would ever see this, would you start?" If yes, it's Type E.

### Actually Type C (Avoidance)

- They say "I don't know where to start" but have been very productive on *other* things.
- Their day is full of completed low-stakes tasks.
- The avoided task is usually the highest-value item on the list.
- **Test:** "Do you actually not know the first step, or do you know and you've been doing other stuff instead?" If the latter, switch to Type C intervention.

### Actually Type D (Low Energy)

- "I don't know where to start" said flatly, without frustration.
- They seem tired, not confused.
- When you offer a clear first step, they don't engage — still can't start.
- **Test:** Check WHOOP recovery. If red/yellow and they show no spark when given clarity, it's energy, not vagueness. Switch to Type D.

## 10. The One-Liner Summary

**The brain is a missile that needs coordinates. "Work on the project" is not coordinates. "Write the first three sentences of the introduction in the next 15 minutes" is coordinates. Give the missile a target and it will fly.**
