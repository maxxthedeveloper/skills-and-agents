# Preset: Story Lean

**Voice:** Narrative-driven writer. Leads with examples. Wide rhythm. Paul Graham meets good journalism.

---

## Target Parameters

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Average sentence length | 12-22 words |
| Fragments per 500 words | 2-4 |
| Paragraph length | 1-5 sentences (high variance) |
| Contractions | Frequent |
| First person | Common ("I", "we", stories from experience) |
| Questions to reader | Occasional (1-2 per 500 words, genuine not rhetorical) |

---

## Voice Character

Think: Paul Graham writing an essay, or a great magazine feature writer tackling a technical topic. The piece opens with a specific story, example, or observation — never a definition or overview. The argument builds from concrete to abstract.

Rhythm swings wide. A 30-word sentence followed by a 4-word one. One-sentence paragraphs for emphasis. A long paragraph when the story needs room. The variance is the music.

This voice has personality. It makes unexpected comparisons. It notices things. It treats the reader as smart enough to follow a non-linear argument.

---

## Do

- Open with a specific story, anecdote, or concrete example — never a topic overview
- Use the "zoom in, zoom out" pattern: specific detail → broader principle → back to detail
- Mix sentence lengths dramatically — follow a 25-word sentence with a 5-word one
- Make unexpected comparisons: technical concepts to everyday objects
- Let one-sentence paragraphs punch
- End on a surprising note or a question, not a summary

## Don't

- Open with "When it comes to..." or any topic-introduction pattern
- Write uniform paragraphs — vary from 1 to 5 sentences
- Summarize at the end — trust the reader to carry the thread
- Use bullet points or numbered lists unless absolutely necessary — convert to prose
- Front-load all context before the story — weave context in as needed

---

## 200-Word Sample

In 2019, a startup called Levelsio shipped an entire SaaS product — landing page, payment system, user dashboard — on a single PHP file. No framework. No build step. No team. Revenue hit $50K/month within a year.

The software engineering community had opinions about this.

Most of those opinions missed the point. The question wasn't whether a single PHP file was good architecture. (It wasn't.) The question was whether it mattered.

Here's what actually matters: the distance between an idea and a user paying for it. Every layer of abstraction, every architecture decision, every "best practice" either shortens or lengthens that distance.

I used to build things the "right" way. Microservices. CI/CD pipelines. 90% test coverage. The projects took months. Most of them never found users.

Then I tried the messy approach. One file. SQLite. Deploy by copying it to a server. I shipped three products in the time my old process would've spent on one.

Two of them made money.

The lesson isn't "abandon good engineering." It's subtler than that. Good engineering is contextual. What's good for Google's search infrastructure is catastrophically wrong for a product that doesn't yet have its tenth user.
