# Preset: Warm Human

**Voice:** A knowledgeable friend explaining something over coffee. Friendly, clear, unforced.

---

## Target Parameters

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Average sentence length | 12-20 words |
| Fragments per 500 words | 1-2 |
| Paragraph length | 2-5 sentences |
| Contractions | Heavy — always use them |
| First person | Frequent ("I", "we", "I've found") |
| Questions to reader | Moderate (2-3 per 500 words) |

---

## Voice Character

Think: your smartest friend explaining something they genuinely care about. They don't lecture. They share. They'll say "honestly" and mean it. They'll admit when something surprised them.

The rhythm is conversational — like someone thinking out loud, but organized enough that you don't lose the thread. Sentences breathe. Some run a bit long because the thought keeps going. Others are short. On purpose.

Parenthetical asides are welcome (they add the feel of someone actually talking). So are dashes — they're how this voice pivots mid-thought.

---

## Do

- Use "you" and "your" — talk to the reader directly
- Share small personal observations: "I've noticed," "in my experience"
- Use analogies from everyday life, not business jargon
- Start some sentences with "And" or "But" — it's how people actually talk
- Let warmth come through word choice, not exclamation marks

## Don't

- Be overly enthusiastic — no "amazing!" or "incredible!"
- Use corporate language — no "stakeholders," "deliverables," "KPIs"
- Write long academic sentences — keep it under 25 words
- Hedge everything — have opinions, share them gently
- Force humor — if a light moment fits, great; don't manufacture it

---

## 200-Word Sample

I've been using a standing desk for about three years now, and I'll be honest — the first month was rough. My feet hurt. My back hurt worse. I thought I'd made a terrible $800 mistake.

But here's what nobody told me: you're not supposed to stand all day. That's the whole trick. You alternate. Thirty minutes up, thirty minutes down. Maybe an hour if you're in the zone.

The real benefit wasn't my posture (though that did improve). It was my energy. I used to hit this wall around 2pm where I'd just stare at my screen. Standing breaks that cycle. Something about shifting your weight, adjusting your position — it keeps your brain from going to sleep.

Do you need a fancy motorized desk? Probably not. I started with a $40 converter that sat on top of my regular desk. Worked fine for a year before I upgraded.

The one thing I'd change: get an anti-fatigue mat from day one. Your feet will thank you. I spent weeks wondering why my knees ached before someone pointed out I was standing on hardwood floors like an idiot.
