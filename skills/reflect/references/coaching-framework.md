# Coaching Framework

Actionable coaching techniques for the reflect skill. Not theory — specific questions, diagnostic patterns, and intervention moves.

---

## The Core Diagnostic: Why Didn't You Get Things Done?

Before asking anything else, classify what you're hearing into one of these patterns:

### Pattern 1: Type B Procrastination (Paul Graham)
**Signal:** They were busy all week but didn't touch the important thing.
**What's happening:** Working hard on less important tasks to avoid the real work. Busyness as a shield against discomfort. The most dangerous type because it feels productive.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "What are the most important problems you're facing? Are you working on one of them? Why not?" (Hamming, via Graham)
- "You got a lot done this week. But was any of it the thing that actually matters?"
- "If you could only keep one thing from this week's work, what would it be? Is that the thing that moves the needle?"

**The schlep blindness test:** "What's the thing you've been avoiding that, if you actually did it, would change everything? What specifically about it feels unpleasant?"

### Pattern 2: Self-Image Thermostat (Maxwell Maltz)
**Signal:** Consistent partial completion. Gets close, then something derails it. Repeating cycle of "almost."
**What's happening:** Their self-image is set to "not quite there." Everything they do, feel, and perform is consistent with who they believe they are. The thermostat resets them to their self-image setting.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "You get close every time. What happens right before it falls apart?"
- "How do you see yourself in relation to this goal? Do you see yourself as someone who actually does this?"
- "What would it mean about you if you actually succeeded? What would change?"
- "Is there a gap between who you'd need to be and who you believe you are?"

**The servo-mechanism reset:**
1. "What would a person who [achieves this] believe about themselves?"
2. "Can you describe what it would feel like — not the achievement, but being that person?"
3. "What's the gap between that person and how you see yourself right now?"

### Pattern 3: Knowledge-Action Gap ("I Know" Syndrome)
**Signal:** "I know what I should do." Accumulated knowledge without implementation. Information hoarding as ego protection.
**What's happening:** Confusing understanding with doing. Theoretical knowledge creates the illusion of progress without the risk of failure.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "You've known this for weeks. Knowing isn't the problem. What is?"
- "What's the difference between knowing you should do it and actually doing it? Where does it break down?"
- "If you know exactly what to do and you're not doing it — what does that tell you about what's actually going on?"

### Pattern 4: External Locus ("Things have been crazy")
**Signal:** Outsourcing agency to circumstances. Everything happens TO them, nothing happens BY them.
**What's happening:** Protecting ego by attributing outcomes to external factors. Avoiding ownership.

**The responsibility ladder:**
1. "What happened?" (Let them tell it)
2. "What was your part in it?" (Introduce ownership gently)
3. "What could you have done differently?" (Open options)
4. "What will you do next time?" (Commit to agency)

**Direct probe:** "You had the same circumstances as everyone else this week. Some people got their thing done. What was different for you?"

### Pattern 5: Meaning Deficit (Jordan Peterson)
**Signal:** "I know I should but I just can't motivate myself." Chronic avoidance without clear fear or external barrier.
**What's happening:** The task isn't connected to anything that matters. They're optimizing for comfort (excessive order) instead of growth (the edge between order and chaos).

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "If you completed this, what would it mean for your life? Take your time."
- "Is this something you want, or something you think you should want?"
- "What gets you out of bed? Not your alarm — what makes you actually want to get up?"
- "What would future-you thank you for doing this week?"

**Peterson's order-chaos diagnostic:**
- Week is 100% predictable → hiding from growth: "Comfortable routines feel safe, but they lead to stagnation. Where's the chaos you're avoiding?"
- Week is 100% chaotic → lost the foundation: "Everything's on fire. But what if the fire is the distraction? What would you do if things were calm?"

### Pattern 6: Shadow Avoidance (Carl Jung)
**Signal:** Strong emotional reaction to the topic. Overexplaining. Denial that seems disproportionate. "I would never..."
**What's happening:** Unintegrated shadow material. The thing they're avoiding reveals something about themselves they don't want to see.

**Approach with care. This is deeper territory.**
- "You seem to have a strong reaction to this. What's underneath that?"
- "What bothers you most about this situation? ... Is there any part of that you recognize in yourself?"
- "What would it mean about you if this is actually what you want?"

### Pattern 7: Physiological Constraint
**Signal:** Low recovery, poor sleep, declining HRV — AND they couldn't focus/execute.
**What's happening:** The system was overloaded. This isn't a psychological problem — it's a body problem. HRV indexes self-regulatory strength (Segerstrom & Nes, 2007). Low HRV = depleted willpower capacity. Sleep restriction reduces cognitive motivation.

**Response:** Validate first. Then intervene on recovery, not mindset.
- "Your recovery was 28% and you slept 5 hours. You didn't lack discipline — you lacked capacity. The tank was empty."
- "HRV has been declining for four days. Your body's self-regulation system is depleted. You literally couldn't will yourself to do it — willpower is physiological, not character."
- "Before we talk about what you should have done, let's talk about what your body needed."

## The Body-Mind Coaching Flow

Always use this decision tree before coaching:

```
1. CHECK THE BODY (WHOOP data)
   ├─ Red/yellow recovery? → Validate. "This wasn't failure — it was physiology."
   │   └─ Intervention = recovery (sleep, strain management, rest)
   └─ Green recovery? → "You had the capacity. Let's explore what happened."
       └─ Go to step 2

2. CHECK THE PATTERN
   ├─ Is this a one-off? → Don't overreact. "One bad day doesn't mean anything."
   └─ Is this recurring? → Which pattern? (1-6 above)
       ├─ Recurring low recovery → Lifestyle problem, not willpower
       └─ Recurring avoidance + good recovery → Psychological pattern

3. EXPLORE THE PSYCHOLOGY (only if body was ready)
   ├─ Self-image check: "How do you see yourself in relation to this?"
   ├─ Meaning check: "Why does this matter to you?"
   ├─ Avoidance check: "What specifically about this feels uncomfortable?"
   └─ Shadow check: "What would it mean about you if you failed? Succeeded?"

4. DESIGN THE INTERVENTION
   ├─ Physiological → Sleep protocol, strain management, rest day
   ├─ Self-image → Identity work, visualization, small wins
   ├─ Meaning → Connect task to values, or honestly let it go
   ├─ Avoidance → Name the fear, shrink the first step
   └─ Accountability → Specific, time-bound, low-barrier commitment
```

## Coaching Moves

### The AWE Question
"And what else?" — Ask this at least once per session. Prevents premature conclusions. The first answer is rarely the real answer.

### The Focus Question
"What's the real challenge here for you?" — The "for you" is critical. Personalizes and prevents deflection into abstractions.

### The Strategic Question
"If you're saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?" — Forces trade-off thinking. Jobs-level focus.

### The Discrepancy Mirror (Motivational Interviewing)
Reflect back the gap between stated values and behavior without judgment:
- "You said health is your top priority. You slept 4.5 hours three nights this week. Help me understand."
- "You said this project matters more than anything. You spent 2 hours on it and 14 on email. What's going on?"

### The Reframe (Jobs-style)
When someone says "I can't" — reframe the stakes:
- "What if not doing this costs you [something they deeply value]? Now can you find a way?"
- The engineer found 28 seconds when Jobs reframed 10 seconds of boot time as "100 human lifecycles per year." Context creates urgency that logic cannot.

### The Contrarian Question (Thiel-style)
"What do you believe about yourself that almost nobody would agree with — but you think is true?"
Surfaces hidden assumptions and self-imposed constraints.

### Rolling with Resistance (MI)
Never fight resistance directly. The impulse to correct triggers more resistance.

- **Amplified reflection:** Slightly exaggerate their resistance. "So there's really no possible way you could fit in 20 minutes?" They'll often correct themselves.
- **Double-sided reflection:** "On one hand, you feel too tired. On the other, you know you always feel better when you do. Both are true." Let the contradiction sit.
- **Reframing:** Change meaning, not facts. "You're not undisciplined — you're exhausted. The question isn't willpower, it's recovery."

### Amplified Reflection (MI)
Slightly exaggerate their resistance so they hear how it sounds:
- They say: "I just couldn't find the time."
- You say: "So there was literally no moment in the entire week where 20 minutes existed?"
- They'll often self-correct. If they double down, there's real information in that too.

### Double-Sided Reflection (MI)
Hold both sides without resolving the tension:
- "On one hand, you're exhausted and need rest. On the other, this is the fourth week you've said that. Both are true. What do you make of that?"
- Let the contradiction sit. Don't resolve it for them.

## The Escalation Ladder

When the same pattern recurs across multiple sessions, static coaching fails. Escalation means changing the intervention intensity, not repeating the same probe louder.

### Level 1 (2nd occurrence): Direct naming
"This showed up last time too. You said [quote from last session]. What changed?"
Simple accountability. Often enough.

### Level 2 (3rd occurrence): Dickens Process
Tony Robbins' pain/pleasure leverage technique. Makes the cost of inaction visceral.

**Pain side (go here first):**
1. "Imagine it's 6 months from now. Same pattern. What has it cost you?"
2. "A year. Two years. Same avoidance. What does your life look like?"
3. "What relationships, opportunities, or goals have you lost?"

**Pleasure side (after pain lands):**
1. "Now — what if you broke this pattern this week?"
2. "Six months of actually doing the thing. What's different?"
3. "What becomes possible that isn't possible now?"

**Key:** Don't rush. Silence after the question is where the work happens. Let them sit with it.

### Level 3 (4th+ occurrence): Identity Intervention
The behavior is a symptom. The identity is the cause.
- "We keep arriving at the same place. The strategy isn't the problem. How do you see yourself in relation to this?"
- "What would a person who doesn't have this pattern believe about themselves?"
- "Is there a version of this goal that actually excites you? Or are we carrying a zombie goal?"

At this level, dropping the goal is a valid and healthy outcome.

## The 6 Human Needs Diagnostic (Tony Robbins)

When a pattern is identified, go one layer deeper: what NEED does the current behavior serve? Every behavior serves at least one of six needs. Understanding which need the avoidance serves reveals the real blocker.

| Need | How avoidance serves it | What you'll hear |
|------|------------------------|------------------|
| **Certainty** | Staying comfortable, avoiding risk | "I'm not ready yet" / "I need more info" |
| **Variety** | Novelty-seeking, system-hopping | "I want to try a new approach" / "This is boring" |
| **Significance** | Being busy = feeling important | "I've been SO busy" / overwork on visible tasks |
| **Connection** | Social activities over solo deep work | "I had to help [person]" / socializing as escape |
| **Growth** | Learning as substitute for doing | "I need to learn X first" / course-hopping |
| **Contribution** | Helping others to avoid own work | "They really needed me" / caretaking |

**How to use:**
- Identify the pattern (1-7 from above)
- Ask: "What does [the avoidance behavior] give you that the hard thing doesn't?"
- Map to the need. The answer reveals the intervention:
  - Certainty-driven → reduce perceived risk: "What's the smallest safe experiment?"
  - Significance-driven → reframe: "Busy isn't important. Impactful is."
  - Growth-driven → call it: "You're learning to avoid doing. What do you already know enough to start?"

Don't lecture about the 6 needs. Use them as an internal diagnostic. The user experiences better questions, not a framework dump.

## Question Bank by Domain

### Productivity & Execution
- "What was supposed to happen since last time? What actually happened?"
- "Walk me through yesterday. Where did the time go?"
- "What did you say yes to that you should have said no to?"
- "What's the one thing that, if you did it, would make everything else easier or unnecessary?"
- "Are you working on the right thing, or just working?"
- "What decisions are you putting off? Those compound."

### Energy & Vitality
- "Rate your energy right now, 1-10. Don't think."
- "Is this tired-from-doing-things or tired-from-existing? Different problems."
- "When did you last feel genuinely energized? Not caffeinated — energized."
- "Compare today to your best day in the last month. What percentage are you at?"

### Sleep
- "What time did you actually get in bed vs when you tried to fall asleep?"
- "What were you doing in the last hour before bed?"
- "Phone in the bedroom?"
- "Do you feel rested or just not tired? There's a difference."

### Stress & Mental Load
- "What's taking up the most mental bandwidth right now?"
- "Is this productive stress or just noise?"
- "What would need to happen for your stress to drop by two points?"
- "What's the thing you're avoiding right now? There's always something."

### Purpose & Meaning (weekly)
- "What are you building right now? Not your to-do list — the bigger thing."
- "If you could only work on one thing for the next month, what would it be?"
- "When did you last feel proud of something you made?"
- "What would you do differently if nothing was expected of you?"

## Red Flags to Listen For

| They say | It usually means | Probe |
|----------|-----------------|-------|
| "I didn't have time" | Prioritization avoidance | "What ate the time?" |
| "I was going to, but..." | Rationalization | "What's the emotion under that explanation?" |
| "I know what I should do" | Knowledge-action gap | "Knowing isn't the problem. What is?" |
| "I'll start Monday" | Temporal discounting | "What's different about Monday-you vs today-you?" |
| "Things have been crazy" | External locus | "What was your part in the chaos?" |
| "I just need the right system" | Tool fetishism | "Three systems this month. The system isn't the problem." |
| Always almost finishing | Self-image thermostat | "How do you see yourself? Someone who finishes?" |
| Busy on wrong things | Type B procrastination | "Was any of that the thing that matters?" |

## Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)

1. **The Advice Monster:** Don't give solutions. Ask questions. "Have you tried X?" is not coaching.
2. **The Validation Trap:** Constant affirmation without challenge. If they haven't exercised in two weeks and recovery is declining, "you're doing your best" is dishonest.
3. **The Drill Sergeant:** All challenge, no support. Triggers shame, which triggers hiding. Hold the standard without shaming.
4. **Drive-By Coaching:** "Your sleep is bad — fix that." Observation without inquiry. They already know. Ask why.
5. **Leading Questions:** "Don't you think you should sleep more?" is not a question. Ask genuinely open questions.
6. **Ignoring the Body:** Coaching mindset when recovery is 15% and sleep was 4 hours. You cannot think your way out of physiological depletion.
