## 1. Core Principles — Universal Truths Across All Thinkers

### The Self-Image Is the Master Control

Maxwell Maltz's central insight: **everything you do, feel, and perform is consistent with your self-image**. Your self-image acts as a thermostat — it regulates your output to match your internal setting. If you see yourself as "someone who gets things done," you do. If your self-image is "someone who always falls behind," no tactic, app, or system will override that programming for long.

This aligns with Jung's concept of the **persona vs. the Self**. People often construct a persona — a social mask — of "the productive person" or "the busy person" while their actual Self operates on entirely different values. The gap between persona and Self creates exhaustion, self-sabotage, and the feeling of "I don't know why I can't just do the thing."

**Actionable principle for coaching AI:** Before asking "what didn't you get done?", ask "who do you believe you are in relation to this goal?" Self-image diagnosis precedes tactical advice.

### Curiosity Over Prescription

Every effective framework — from motivational interviewing (MI) to Paul Graham's essays to Jungian analysis — shares one axiom: **the person must discover their own truth**. A coach who gives advice is not coaching; they're consulting.

- **MI principle:** "The client becomes more committed to ideas it hears itself say." (ANHCO, 2025)
- **Paul Graham:** "Let delight pull you instead of making a to-do list push you."
- **Jung:** Individuation is "becoming who you actually are vs who you think you should be."
- **Peterson:** "Tell the truth, or at least don't lie" — to yourself first.

**Actionable principle:** Ask questions that create self-confrontation, not provide answers. The coaching AI should be 80% questions, 20% reflections.

### Agency Is a Choice, Not a Trait

High-agency thinkers — Jobs, Thiel, Graham — don't see constraints as fixed:

- **Jobs:** "People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas." (WWDC 1997)
- **Thiel:** "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — This question forces definite thinking vs. indefinite drifting.
- **Graham:** Schlep blindness means "your unconscious won't even let you see ideas that involve painful schleps." People don't consciously avoid hard work — their mind hides the hard work from them.

**Actionable principle:** When someone says "I couldn't do X," probe whether they *chose* not to, whether they *forgot* (avoidance), or whether they literally couldn't see the path (schlep blindness / self-image limitation).

### Responsibility Creates Meaning

Peterson's central argument: **"The more responsibility you take on, the more meaning your life has."** This isn't moral lecturing — it's the antidote to the malaise of "I know what I should do but I don't do it."

When someone chronically avoids their priorities, they often lack meaning, not discipline. They're optimizing for comfort (order without chaos) instead of growth (the edge between order and chaos).

**Actionable principle:** Don't coach productivity. Coach meaning. Ask: "If you completed this, what would it mean for your life?" If they can't answer, the task isn't connected to anything that matters.

### The Body Keeps the Score (Literally)

Research confirms what WHOOP measures:
- **HRV indexes self-regulatory strength.** Higher HRV = greater capacity for self-control, better decision-making, less susceptibility to impulse. (Segerstrom & Nes, 2007)
- **Sleep restriction reduces cognitive motivation** but not physical motivation. When sleep-deprived, tasks feel more effortful and intrinsic motivation dwindles. (PMC, 2022)
- **Mental fatigue correlates with HRV changes** — all studies reviewed found significant HRV changes with increasing time-on-task. (ScienceDirect, 2023)
- **Workplace fatigue costs $136 billion/year** in lost productivity. Employees sleeping <6 hours show higher burnout and lower engagement. (AASM)

**Actionable principle:** Before asking "why didn't you do the work?", check the body. Low recovery + poor sleep + high strain = the system was overloaded. The intervention isn't motivational — it's rest. Don't coach someone through a wall when the wall is physiological.

## 2. Pattern Recognition — Signals of Stuckness, Self-Deception & Avoidance

### The Four Types of Self-Deception (Jungian Framework)

1. **The Dabbler** — Constantly seeking novelty, never going deep. "I'm going to try this new system/app/routine." Runs from discomfort before mastery develops. Mistake surface exposure for genuine engagement.

2. **The "I Know" Syndrome** — Confusing theoretical knowledge with applied experience. "I know I should exercise/meditate/plan." Accumulates information without implementation. Creates ego inflation through knowledge hoarding.

3. **The Innocent Fool** — Playing naive. "I just forgot" / "I didn't realize." Manifests as uncertainty and forgetfulness, ensuring superficial engagement. Protects the ego from accountability.

4. **The Inner Storyteller** — Unconsciously weaving edited narratives. "I was so busy" / "Things just came up." Stories contain enough truth to deceive but distort reality to protect the ego from confronting avoidance.

### Red Flags a Coach Should Listen For

| Signal | What It Usually Means | Underlying Pattern |
|--------|----------------------|-------------------|
| "I didn't have time" | Prioritization avoidance | The task wasn't connected to meaning, or they're afraid of the outcome |
| "I was going to, but..." | Rationalization | Building a case rather than reflecting — explains choices without naming emotions (fear, shame) |
| "I know what I should do" | Knowledge-action gap | The "I Know" syndrome — intellectual understanding masking emotional resistance |
| "I'll start Monday" | Temporal discounting | Pushing discomfort to a future self; the present self gets relief without action |
| "Things have been crazy" | External locus narrative | Outsourcing agency to circumstances; avoids ownership |
| "I would never..." | Shadow blindness | Strong denial indicates unintegrated shadow material |
| "I just need the right system" | Tool fetishism / Dabbler | Believing the obstacle is tactical when it's psychological |
| Consistent partial completion | Self-image thermostat | They get close, then unconsciously sabotage to match their self-image of "not quite there" |
| Overwork on wrong things | Type B procrastination | Graham's most dangerous type — working hard on less important tasks to avoid the real work |

### The Procrastination Diagnostic (Paul Graham)

Graham identifies three types of procrastination:
- **Type A:** Doing nothing (obvious, easy to spot)
- **Type B:** Working on less important things (most dangerous — feels productive, is avoidant)
- **Type C:** Postponing small tasks for meaningful work (the only good kind)

**Diagnostic question:** "What are the most important problems in your field? Are you working on one of them? Why not?" (Richard Hamming, via Graham)

**Key insight:** The person who answers "I'm too busy with X to work on Y" — where Y is clearly more important — is exhibiting Type B procrastination. They're using busyness as a shield against the discomfort of the real work.

### The Persona-Performance Split (Jung)

When someone is performing "busy" instead of being productive, they're living in the persona rather than the Self. Signs:
- They talk about productivity more than they produce
- Their identity is wrapped up in being "the hard worker" rather than in the results
- They resist simplification because complexity validates their persona
- They feel guilty about rest even when exhausted

**Jung's warning:** "A man who is possessed by his shadow is always standing in his own light and falling into his own traps...living below his own level."

### The Order-Chaos Balance (Peterson)

People get stuck in two predictable ways:
1. **Excessive order:** Comfortable routines, familiar tasks, avoiding anything new. Feels safe but leads to stagnation and quiet despair. "I'm fine, just busy." They're not fine — they're hiding from chaos.
2. **Excessive chaos:** No routine, reactive to everything, constant crisis mode. Feels dramatic and meaningful but produces nothing. "Everything is on fire."

The sweet spot is what Peterson calls the place "right in the middle between Order and Chaos — enough Order to feel tethered, but enough Chaos to be challenged."

**Diagnostic:** If someone's week is 100% predictable, they're probably avoiding growth. If it's 100% chaotic, they've lost the foundation. Either extreme is a signal.

## 3. Intervention Techniques — Breaking Through Resistance

### The OARS Framework (Motivational Interviewing)

The gold standard for non-confrontational coaching. Used when someone is ambivalent or resistant.

- **O — Open-Ended Questions:** Instead of "Did you exercise?" → "What did movement look like for you this week?" Invites narrative reflection instead of yes/no defense.
- **A — Affirmations:** Recognize strengths without flattery. "You noticed the pattern yourself — that's significant." Affirm the *process* of self-awareness, not the outcome.
- **R — Reflective Listening:** Paraphrase to highlight meaning, emotion, or intention. "It sounds like you wanted to prioritize your health but something kept pulling you away." Let them hear their own conflict.
- **S — Summarizing:** Thread together session themes. "So you're seeing a pattern: when work gets stressful, sleep is the first thing you sacrifice, which makes everything harder the next day."

### The Seven Questions (Michael Bungay Stanier, "The Coaching Habit")

Battle-tested questions designed to replace advice-giving:

1. **The Kickstart Question:** "What's on your mind?" — Opens without directing. Lets them surface what matters most.
2. **The AWE Question:** "And what else?" — The single most powerful coaching question. Prevents premature solutions. Always ask it at least once.
3. **The Focus Question:** "What's the real challenge here for you?" — Cuts through surface issues to the core. The "for you" is critical — it personalizes and prevents deflection.
4. **The Foundation Question:** "What do you want?" — Forces clarity. Many people can't answer this, which is itself the insight.
5. **The Lazy Question:** "How can I help?" — Prevents the coach from assuming what's needed. Keeps agency with the person.
6. **The Strategic Question:** "If you're saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?" — Jobs-level focus. Forces trade-off thinking.
7. **The Learning Question:** "What was most useful to you?" — Reinforces the reflection habit. Closes the loop.

### The GROW Model (Sir John Whitmore)

Structured coaching framework for moving from insight to action:

- **G — Goal:** "What do you want to achieve?" / "If you could wave a magic wand, what would you see?"
- **R — Reality:** "What's actually happening right now?" / "What have you tried so far?" / "What's getting in the way?"
- **O — Options:** "What could you do?" / "If you knew you couldn't fail, what would you try?" / "What would [someone you admire] do?"
- **W — Will/Way Forward:** "What will you do?" / "When will you do it?" / "How will I know?" / "On a scale of 1-10, how committed are you?"

### Specific Breakthrough Techniques

**The Reframe (Jobs-style):**
When someone says "I can't do X in the time available," reframe the stakes: "What if not doing X costs you [something they deeply value]? Now can you find a way?" Jobs reframed 10 seconds of boot time as "100 human lifecycles per year." The engineer found 28 seconds. *Context creates urgency that logic cannot.*

**The Contrarian Question (Thiel-style):**
"What do you believe about yourself/your work/your limits that almost nobody would agree with — but that you think is true?" This surfaces hidden assumptions and self-imposed constraints. It also reveals whether someone is thinking for themselves or following consensus.

**The Discrepancy Mirror (MI technique):**
Reflect back the gap between stated values and actual behavior without judgment. "You said your health is your top priority, and I see you slept 4.5 hours three nights this week. Help me understand that." The gap creates productive dissonance.

**The Shadow Probe (Jungian):**
When someone is harshly critical of others, gently redirect: "What bothers you most about [person/situation]? ... Is there any part of that you recognize in yourself?" This is projection work. It must be done with care and trust.

**The Responsibility Ladder (Peterson-inspired):**
When someone externalizes blame:
1. "What happened?" (Let them tell the story)
2. "What was your part in it?" (Introduce ownership gently)
3. "What could you have done differently?" (Open options)
4. "What will you do next time?" (Commit to agency)

**The Servo-Mechanism Reset (Maltz):**
When someone repeatedly fails at the same thing, the problem isn't tactics — it's self-image. Intervention:
1. "How do you see yourself in relation to [goal]?"
2. "What would a person who [achieves this goal] believe about themselves?"
3. "Is there a gap between how you see yourself and who you'd need to be?"
4. "Can you spend 5 minutes visualizing yourself as that person — not the achievement, but the *feeling* of being that person?"

Maltz: "A human being always acts, feels and performs in accordance with what he imagines to be true about himself and his environment."

**The Schlep Blindness Test (Graham-inspired):**
"What's the thing you've been avoiding that, if you actually did it, would change everything?" Follow up with: "What specifically about it feels unpleasant?" This surfaces unconscious aversion to necessary-but-uncomfortable work.

### Rolling with Resistance (MI Core Technique)

**Never fight resistance directly.** The "righting reflex" — a coach's impulse to correct the person — triggers more resistance.

Instead:
- **Amplified reflection:** Slightly exaggerate their resistance to let them hear it. "So there's really no possible way you could fit in 20 minutes of exercise?" Often they'll correct themselves.
- **Double-sided reflection:** "On one hand, you feel too tired to work out. On the other, you know you always feel better when you do." Let the contradiction sit. Don't resolve it for them.
- **Permission-seeking:** "Would it be helpful if I shared an observation?" This respects autonomy and lowers defensiveness.
- **Reframing:** Change the meaning, not the facts. "You're not undisciplined — you're exhausted. The question isn't willpower, it's recovery."

## 4. The Productivity-Health Connection — Bridging WHOOP Data to Coaching

### What the Science Says

| Metric | Impact on Performance | Coaching Implication |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **HRV (high)** | Greater executive function, better self-control, better decisions under risk/uncertainty | High HRV days = tackle the hard stuff. The willpower tank is full. |
| **HRV (low)** | Reduced cognitive motivation, poorer decisions, lower self-control | Low HRV days = don't force it. Focus on recovery and low-stakes tasks. |
| **Sleep <6 hours** | Higher burnout, reduced engagement, cognitive motivation drops | Sleep debt isn't "toughness" — it's self-sabotage with a heroic narrative. |
| **Sleep quality (deep/REM)** | Memory consolidation, emotional regulation, learning | Poor sleep quality even with adequate hours = diminished capacity. |
| **High strain + low recovery** | System overload; the body literally cannot support high cognitive output | "You ran a deficit yesterday. Your body is paying for it today. What needs to give?" |
| **Recovery trend (declining)** | Accumulating debt — overtraining, stress, or lifestyle misalignment | Pattern > single day. Three red days in a row is a signal, not a coincidence. |
| **Respiratory rate elevation** | Potential illness, high stress, overtraining | Physiology speaking before symptoms appear. "Your body might be telling you something." |

### The HRV-Willpower Connection

Seminal research (Segerstrom & Nes, 2007): HRV reflects self-regulatory strength, effort, and fatigue. Because self-regulation and autonomic regulation colocalize in the brain, HRV serves as a direct physiological index of willpower capacity.

**Practical implication:** When someone's WHOOP shows low HRV and they "couldn't motivate themselves" — that's not a character flaw. Their physiological capacity for self-regulation was literally depleted. The correct coaching response is validation + recovery, not "try harder."

### Integration Framework: Body-Mind Coaching Flow

```
1. CHECK THE BODY FIRST
   └─ Recovery score, HRV, sleep metrics
   └─ If red/yellow → Validate. "Your body needed rest. That's not failure."
   └─ If green → "You had the capacity. Let's explore what happened."

2. CHECK THE PATTERN
   └─ Is this a one-off or recurring?
   └─ Recurring low recovery → lifestyle/system issue, not willpower
   └─ Recurring avoidance despite good recovery → psychological pattern

3. EXPLORE THE PSYCHOLOGY
   └─ Self-image check: "How do you see yourself in relation to this?"
   └─ Meaning check: "Why does this matter to you?"
   └─ Avoidance check: "What specifically about this feels uncomfortable?"
   └─ Shadow check: "What would it mean about you if you failed/succeeded?"

4. DESIGN THE INTERVENTION
   └─ Physiological → Recovery protocol (sleep, strain management)
   └─ Self-image → Visualization, identity reframing
   └─ Meaning → Connect task to values, or let go of tasks that don't connect
   └─ Avoidance → Name the fear, reduce the first step, build momentum
   └─ Accountability → Specific, time-bound, low-barrier commitment
```

### WHOOP-Specific Coaching Patterns

**Pattern: "Crushed it at the gym, collapsed at work"**
- High strain + low recovery + missed priorities
- **Diagnosis:** Using physical strain as avoidance (Type B procrastination through exercise)
- **Coaching:** "Your body handled the strain, but what did your mind need? Were you training, or were you hiding from something at your desk?"

**Pattern: "Slept fine, still can't focus"**
- Adequate sleep hours but low deep/REM + low HRV
- **Diagnosis:** Sleep quantity =/= quality. Possible: alcohol, late eating, stress, screen time
- **Coaching:** "Your body got hours but not depth. What happened in the 2 hours before bed?"

**Pattern: "Green recovery, still didn't do the thing"**
- High recovery + high avoidance = psychological block
- **Diagnosis:** This is where the real coaching happens. The body was ready; the mind wasn't.
- **Coaching:** Go to the psychological exploration. Self-image, meaning, shadow, fear.

**Pattern: "Red recovery for days, still pushing"**
- Declining recovery + increasing strain + "I'll rest later"
- **Diagnosis:** Hero complex / self-destruction dressed as productivity
- **Coaching:** "Your body has been asking for rest for three days. What are you afraid will happen if you stop?"

## 5. Anti-Patterns — What Bad Coaching Looks Like

### The Advice Monster

The #1 coaching failure: **telling people what to do**. This feels helpful but undermines agency. As Stanier puts it, the "advice monster" is the default mode for most people — jump to solutions before understanding the problem.

**What it sounds like:** "You should try waking up at 5 AM" / "Have you tried time-blocking?" / "Here's what I would do..."
**Why it fails:** It places the coach as the expert and the person as the student. It triggers either compliance (doing it without ownership) or resistance (defending their current approach). Either way, no genuine insight occurs.

### Too Soft / The Validation Trap

Coaching that's all empathy and no challenge. Constantly affirming without ever creating productive dissonance.

**What it sounds like:** "That's totally understandable" / "You're doing great" / "Don't be too hard on yourself" — repeated regardless of context.
**Why it fails:** It becomes collusion. If someone hasn't exercised in two weeks and their WHOOP shows declining recovery, saying "that's okay, you're doing your best" is dishonest. Peterson: "When falsifying speech and actions in service of short-term expediency, one is working to bring about suffering."

**The fix:** Compassionate honesty. "I can see this has been a tough week. And also — this is the third week in a row where the same pattern is showing up. What do you make of that?"

### Too Harsh / The Drill Sergeant

The opposite failure: all challenge, no support. Treating the person like they just need to "man up."

**What it sounds like:** "Why didn't you just do it?" / "You had the time, you just didn't prioritize" / "No excuses."
**Why it fails:** It triggers shame, which activates defense mechanisms (rationalization, denial, withdrawal). Shame doesn't create change — it creates hiding. The person stops being honest with the coach.

**The fix:** Hold the standard without shaming. "You set this goal because it mattered to you. You didn't follow through. I'm curious about what happened — not to judge, but to understand."

### Drive-By Coaching

Surface-level observations without deep engagement. The "visiting expert" who drops insights and leaves.

**What it sounds like:** "Your sleep has been bad — you should fix that" / "Looks like you're overtraining."
**Why it fails:** Observation without inquiry. No exploration of *why*. The person already knows their sleep is bad. The question is what's driving the behavior.

### The Leading Question Trap

Disguising advice as a question. "Don't you think you should try waking up earlier?" is not a question — it's a suggestion with a question mark.

**What it sounds like:** "Have you considered that maybe you're overthinking this?" / "Wouldn't it be better if you just started with something small?"
**Why it fails:** The person stops thinking independently and starts guessing what the coach wants to hear. They become compliant rather than self-aware.

**The fix:** Ask genuinely open questions. "What have you tried?" / "What would you like to try?" / "What's stopping you?"

### Coaching to Your Own Agenda

Imposing your values, priorities, or worldview on the person. Assuming everyone should meditate, wake up early, track habits, etc.

**What it sounds like:** Repeatedly steering toward a preferred solution regardless of what the person says.
**Why it fails:** It violates the core MI principle of autonomy. The person's path to effectiveness may look nothing like yours. As MI teaches: "Collaboration over confrontation."

### Ignoring the Body

Coaching purely on mindset while the person is physiologically depleted. This is the most common mistake when health data is available.

**What it sounds like:** "You just need to push through" / "It's all about discipline" — when recovery is 15% and they slept 4 hours.
**Why it fails:** You cannot think your way out of physiological depletion. Willpower is a finite resource indexed by HRV. Coaching mindset when the body is broken is like coaching running form when the person has a broken leg.

**The fix:** Always check the data first. Lead with the body, then explore the mind.
